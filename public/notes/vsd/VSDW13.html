1.1 What Makes These Domains Unique?

Audience Characteristics

When creating visualizations for journalism and public policy, we must consider a diverse audience that includes:

  • General Public - People without specialized domain knowledge
  • Policymakers - Decision-makers who need clear, actionable insights
  • Non-expert Readers - Individuals seeking to understand complex issues
The general public may not be domain experts, but we need to communicate information effectively to them. If data comes from a specialized domain, we must simplify it so that the general public can grasp the key messages easily.

Primary Goals

Goal Description
Simplify Complex Data Transform technical or complicated datasets into accessible visual stories
Reveal Trends Make patterns and changes over time immediately visible
Ensure Transparency Maintain clarity about data sources and methodology

Critical Constraints

When designing visualizations for journalism and public policy, we must adhere to strict constraints:

Constraint Why It Matters
Avoid Misinformation Any slight shift in perspective can turn information into misinformation. We must present facts accurately without distortion.
No Scale Manipulation Manipulating axes or scales can mislead viewers about the magnitude of changes or differences.
Mobile-Friendly Design Most of the general public accesses content on mobile devices with varying screen sizes.
Accessibility Visualizations must work across different bandwidths, devices, and accessibility needs (24/7 global access).
Narrative Structure Must tell a coherent story with connected parts, not just present disconnected charts.
Sometimes when we try to fit data into visualization, we might use factual data, but the visualization can give a different impression to the audience. We need to be very careful about these constraints. When presenting data on small screens, we cannot expect our audience to always use large displays, so mobile-friendly and accessible design is crucial.

Narrative Visualisation Frameworks

Scrollytelling: Step-by-Step Narrative

Scrollytelling combines two concepts:

  • Scroll - The physical action of scrolling on a device
  • Storytelling - A narrative that unfolds progressively

In scrollytelling, as users scroll through content on their devices, they navigate through a story. The visualization changes dynamically based on scroll position, creating an interactive narrative experience. This technique is particularly effective for mobile and small-screen devices.

This technique became very popular because when people scroll on their devices, they're progressing through a story in a very interactive manner. The visualization updates automatically as you scroll, making the experience intuitive and engaging.

Real-World Examples

Example 1: New York Times - Classroom Ventilation During COVID-19

The New York Times created an interactive article titled "How Classroom Design Should Have Open Windows or Air Flow" during the COVID-19 pandemic. This is an excellent example of scrollytelling in journalism.

Key Features of the Visualization:

Initial Setup:

  • Shows a classroom with an instructor and students
  • Each desk is 6 feet apart (social distancing)
  • Students are wearing masks
  • Visualization demonstrates airflow patterns without requiring text reading

Scenario 1 - Closed Windows:

  • Airflow visualization shows circulation within the closed room
  • Each person's breathing creates localized airflow centers (visible near mouths)
  • One infected student is introduced (first row, first column)
  • Legend appears: "Concentration of Contaminants" (yellow = less, red = more)
  • Animation shows contamination spreading throughout the classroom over time
  • Initially low concentration becomes uniformly high across the entire room
Notice how just by scrolling, without reading any text, you can understand the story. The visualization itself communicates the danger of poor ventilation. You can see how one infected person can contaminate the entire classroom when windows are closed.

Scenario 2 - Window Open:

  • Right-side window opens
  • Airflow pattern changes dramatically
  • Fresh air enters and circulates differently
  • Contamination spread is significantly reduced
  • High concentration limited to specific areas

Scenario 3 - Exhaust Fan + Air Cleaner:

  • Exhaust fan installed at back of room
  • Air cleaner added to the setup
  • Fan pushes room air outside (unlike Scenario 2 where it brought fresh air in)
  • Air cleaner continuously filters surrounding air
  • Most of classroom remains at moderate or low contamination levels
  • Instructor area shows reduced exposure compared to Scenario 1

Cross-Sectional View:

The visualization also provides cross-sectional views showing contamination density at different heights in the room:

  • Scenario 1 (Closed): Instructor most vulnerable; corner students have higher concentration due to airflow obstruction
  • Scenario 2 (Window Open): Front area only shows high density; concentration restricted from infected student to instructor level
  • Scenario 3 (Fan + Cleaner): Most balanced distribution with lowest overall contamination
This visualization is a brilliant example of how simulation combined with interactive scrollytelling can help people understand complex scenarios like ventilation and filtration working with social distancing and masking. It creates a closed-room crowd scenario using a school design as an example.

Example 2: New York Times - Weather Visualization

Another New York Times interactive piece focuses on weather data, specifically explaining why Chicago experienced cold waves as extreme as the Arctic.

Visualization Elements:

  • Global Wind Patterns: Shows airflow at equatorial and polar regions
  • Wind Speed Coding: Red/pink = slower wind speed; Green = faster wind speed
  • Local Airflows: Three distinct local air flows with varying speeds
  • Cold Wave Direction: Animated arrows showing cold air movement
  • Temperature Gradient: Warmer air below navigation line, cold air above
  • Jet Streams: Fast-moving air currents shown with speed indicators

The visualization demonstrates how changing airflow directions and jet stream patterns caused Chicago to experience Arctic-level cold temperatures. The interactive scrolling format makes complex meteorological concepts accessible to general readers.

These two examples briefly explain how important it is to have good visualization in the journalism domain. Scrollytelling allows the story to progress in a very interactive manner while scrolling, making complex information digestible for everyone.

Case Study 2: Election Maps ‚Äì Misleading vs. Balanced Visualization

Election maps are commonly used in journalism, but they can be highly misleading if not designed correctly.

The Problem with Traditional Election Maps:

A typical 2016 U.S. election map shows geographical areas colored red or blue based on which party won:

Limitations of Basic Electoral Maps:

  • Ignores population density: Shows only which party received more votes in each area
  • Binary representation: Entire state appears to belong to one party, but not 100% of people vote for the same party
  • No vote share information: Doesn't show what percentage of votes went to each party
  • Geographic distortion: Large but sparsely populated areas appear more significant

Improved Approach:

  • Gradient coloring: Use color gradients to show vote share variations
  • Population-adjusted: Consider state population in the visualization
  • Vote share visualization: Show how votes are distributed from blue to red spectrum
  • Nuanced representation: Display the actual distribution rather than winner-takes-all approach
Each of these visualizations tells a different story. The improved version doesn't just show which party won which state, but shows how the share of votes is distributed. This gives a much more accurate picture of public opinion rather than the misleading impression that entire states are uniformly red or blue.

1.5 Activity: Fix the Bad Chart

This activity demonstrates how the same visualization technique with different data presentations can lead to completely opposite conclusions.

The Problem: Poverty Maps

Bad Chart - Absolute Numbers:

  • Shows total count of people in poverty for each state
  • Large, populous states like California, Texas, Florida, and New York appear darkest
  • Creates impression that these states have the most severe poverty issues
  • Small states with high poverty rates appear lighter and seem less problematic

Why This Is Misleading:

  • States with larger populations naturally have higher absolute poverty counts
  • Doesn't account for state population differences
  • Fails to show poverty intensity or rate
  • Small states with serious poverty problems appear better off
The visualization problem here is that row counts are being used for the coloring. The effect is that large and populous states are colored with the darkest colors. They have higher populations, so they might have higher absolute numbers of people in poverty. But percentage-wise, it might not be the same at all.

Good Chart - Poverty Rate (Percentage):

  • Shows percentage of population in poverty
  • Previously "worst" states now show moderate poverty rates
  • Some small states now appear as having higher poverty intensity
  • Provides accurate comparison of poverty severity across states

Key Insight:

  • States with less population but high poverty percentage were hidden in absolute count visualization
  • Large states may have high absolute numbers but lower poverty rates
  • Using relative data (percentages) reveals the true geographic distribution of poverty intensity
When we start showing the percentage poverty, it makes much more sense. Otherwise, states with less population will have high percentages of poverty but won't be highlighted in absolute count visualizations. The same visualization technique produces completely different messages depending on whether we use absolute or relative data. This is why choosing the right data representation is critical.

Lessons Learned:

Principle Application
Use Relative Metrics When comparing across different population sizes, use rates/percentages instead of absolute counts
Consider Context Population size, geographic area, and other contextual factors matter
Question Your Data Always ask: "Is this the right way to present this information?"
Avoid Misleading Patterns The same technique with different data representations can mislead audiences

üí° Practice Questions

Q1: What is scrollytelling and why is it particularly effective for journalism?
Scrollytelling combines scrolling action with storytelling, creating an interactive narrative where visualizations update automatically as users scroll. It's effective for journalism because it works well on mobile devices, keeps audiences engaged, and allows complex stories to unfold progressively without overwhelming viewers.
Q2: Why should election maps show gradient colors instead of binary red/blue coloring?
Gradient colors show vote share distribution more accurately. Binary coloring suggests 100% of a state voted one way, which is never true. Gradients reveal that votes are distributed across parties, providing a nuanced and honest representation of public opinion rather than winner-takes-all simplification.
Q3: What's the difference between absolute numbers and relative metrics in poverty visualization?
Absolute numbers show total count of people in poverty, which makes large-population states appear worse. Relative metrics (poverty rate/percentage) show the proportion of population in poverty, revealing true poverty intensity regardless of state size. Relative metrics are more meaningful for comparison.
Q4: Explain the Martini Glass Structure in data visualization.
The Martini Glass Structure represents data flow: wide end (input) shows multiple data sources converging, narrow stem represents data filtering and transformation, and the output end shows insights expanding into visualizations. It provides a framework for organizing how data moves through the visualization process.
Q5: What are Sankey diagrams best used for?
Sankey diagrams excel at showing flow and resource allocation, particularly for budgets. The width of flows represents quantity, making it easy to see proportions and how resources move from one category to another. They're ideal for visualizing budget breakdowns, revenue flows, and application processes.

2.1 Nature of Scientific Data

Scientific visualization differs significantly from journalism visualization because of the unique characteristics of scientific data and its expert audience.

Key Characteristics of Scientific Data

Characteristic Description Implication
High-Dimensional Contains many attributes (typically 6-7 or more) Requires specialized visualization techniques to represent multiple dimensions simultaneously
Complex & Multivariate Multiple variables interact with each other Need to show relationships between variables, not just individual data points
Requires Precision Sub-decimal points, scientific notation for very large/small numbers Visualizations must maintain accuracy and not round inappropriately
Reproducible Same experiment should yield same data following same steps Visualization methodology must be documented and replicable
Expert Audience Both creators and readers are domain experts Can use technical terminology and specialized visual forms
Generally, anything having more than 6-7 attributes is considered high dimensional. Scientific data is not just high dimensional for each variable, but we can have multiple variables, making it complex and multivariate. The readers are assumed to be domain experts, so both the creator and readers understand the specialized language and visual conventions of their field.

Comparison: Journalism vs. Scientific Visualization

Aspect Journalism Scientific Research
Audience General public Domain experts
Data Complexity Simplified, 1-3 variables High-dimensional, multivariate
Precision Required Approximate, rounded Exact, with scientific notation
Visual Style Engaging, storytelling-focused Technical, information-dense
Goal Communicate insights to non-experts Enable detailed analysis and peer review

2.2 Visualisation Types Across Sciences

Bioinformatics Visualizations

Bioinformatics deals with large-scale genome data and biological information that requires specialized visualization techniques.

1. Genome Browsers (UCSC, Ensembl)

  • Purpose: Navigate and explore genetic data
  • Features: Shows genes in relation to each other across different species
  • Example: Comparing human genes with Chimpanzee, Gorilla, and other species
  • Interactive elements: Select specific species to see genetic relationships
  • Visual structure: Hierarchical, tree-like organization
In the genome browser example, you can see genes on the left side being shown in relation to each other. Different species have some gene expressions that are common and others that are unique. This shows what genetic material we as humans share with Chimpanzees, Gorillas, and other species. You can select a particular species and see how they're related - it looks like a hierarchical organogram.

2. Multiple Alignment Visualization

  • Compares DNA/protein sequences across multiple organisms
  • Highlights conserved regions and mutations
  • Uses color coding to indicate similarity/difference

3. Protein Structures (PyMOL, Chimera)

  • 3D molecular visualization: Shows spatial arrangement of atoms
  • Binding sites: Visualizes how molecules bind to receptors
  • Interactive rotation: View structures from different angles
  • Color coding: Different molecular components shown in distinct colors
Tools like PyMOL and Chimera allow you to specify parameters and get visualizations. These visualizations capture the way molecules and internal structures are organized. You can see how DNA or receptors get bound to molecules - showing receptor-molecule binding interactions.

4. Phylogenetic Trees

  • Purpose: Show evolutionary relationships between species
  • Types: Can be rooted (single origin) or unrooted (network)
  • Super families: May show cross-connections between lineages
  • Additional information: Molecular arrangements shown with color coding at nodes
[Insert diagram: Phylogenetic tree showing evolutionary relationships with molecular arrangements at nodes]

5. Heatmaps of Gene Expression

  • Purpose: Identify which genes respond to diseases or drugs
  • Structure: Genes as rows, samples as columns
  • Color intensity: Shows expression level (high/low)
  • Application: Pinpoint genes responsible for particular symptoms
  • Clustering: Groups similar expression patterns together
Heatmaps show how many active genes are there which are responding or responsible (or not responding) to a particular disease and drugs. It shows how these genes are being used across multiple samples to pinpoint which genes are responsible for particular expressions or symptoms.

Physics & Engineering Visualizations

1. Signal Plots (Waveforms)

2.4 Integrity and Reproducibility

Scientific visualizations must maintain the highest standards of integrity and be fully reproducible by other researchers.

Key Requirements for Scientific Integrity

Requirement Description Why It Matters
Clear Axes and Units Every axis must be labeled with precise units (e.g., mg/L, ¬∞C, meters) Prevents misinterpretation and allows accurate comparison across studies
Documentation of Preprocessing Document all data cleaning, filtering, and transformation steps Enables others to replicate the analysis and verify results
Avoiding Cherry-Picking Present all relevant data, not just results that support hypothesis Prevents bias and maintains scientific honesty
Open Data Policies Make raw data available for peer review and validation Supports transparency and allows independent verification

Cherry-Picking: A Critical Issue

Cherry-picking refers to selectively using only part of the data to highlight something that may not be generally true when looking at the complete dataset.

Example Scenario:

  • A drug trial shows positive results in weeks 2-4
  • But weeks 1, 5, and 6 show negative or neutral results
  • Publishing only weeks 2-4 data is cherry-picking
  • Complete data must be shown for honest scientific communication
Cherry-picking is when we use a part of data to highlight something which may not be generally true if you look at all the data. This is a serious integrity issue in scientific visualization. We discussed this earlier in the course - you must present the complete picture, not just the parts that support your conclusion.

Best Practices Checklist

  • ‚úì Include scale bars and legends for all visual elements
  • ‚úì Specify statistical methods used
  • ‚úì Indicate error bars and confidence intervals
  • ‚úì Provide data source citations
  • ‚úì Document software versions and parameters
  • ‚úì Include negative results, not just positive findings
  • ‚úì Make raw data accessible through repositories
  • ‚úì Follow domain-specific visualization standards

  • Medical imaging converts raw scans (MRI/CT) into interactive, meaningful visualizations
  • GIS tools allow layering multiple spatial data types on geographic maps
  • Scientific integrity requires clear axes, documented preprocessing, avoiding cherry-picking, and open data policies
  • Python (Matplotlib, Seaborn, Plotly, Bokeh) and R (ggplot2, Shiny) are primary scientific visualization tools

üí° Practice Questions

Q1: What makes scientific data "high-dimensional" and why does this matter?
High-dimensional data has 6-7 or more attributes/variables. This matters because it requires specialized visualization techniques to represent multiple dimensions simultaneously while maintaining clarity and allowing experts to see complex relationships between variables.
Q2: How do genome browsers help researchers understand genetic relationships?
Genome browsers visualize genes in relation to each other across different species, showing which genetic expressions are common and which are unique. Researchers can select specific species to see evolutionary relationships and shared genetic material in a hierarchical, interactive format.
Q3: What information can you infer from temperature anomaly time series since 1850?
The time series shows temperatures below baseline until 1930s, then rising with some fluctuations due to global warming dynamics, and finally a clear upward trend after global warming accelerated, reaching +1.3¬∞C above baseline today - the highest ever recorded.
Q4: Explain what "cherry-picking" means in scientific visualization and why it's problematic.
Cherry-picking is selectively using only part of the data to highlight results that support a hypothesis, ignoring contradictory data. It's problematic because it creates bias, misrepresents reality, and violates scientific integrity by preventing others from seeing the complete picture.
Q5: How do GIS tools like QGIS and ArcGIS work with layers?
GIS tools start with a base geographic map, then allow users to add multiple data layers (satellite imagery, weather data, custom datasets) on top. Each layer adds new information while maintaining geographic context, creating rich, multi-dimensional spatial visualizations.

Key Concepts in BI Visualization

KPIs (Key Performance Indicators)

KPIs are quantifiable metrics that indicate how effectively an organization is achieving key business objectives.

  • Focus: Show only the most critical metrics, not every data point
  • Examples: Average turnover, quarterly revenue, customer acquisition cost, retention rate
  • Purpose: Enable quick assessment of business health
  • Presentation: Often displayed prominently with sparklines or gauges
We should focus on key performance indicators in our visualization. We don't need to pay attention to each individual data point. What are the key performance indicators? It might be average turnover, quarterly turnover - these are metrics we should keep in our visualizations.

Metrics vs. Insights

Metrics Insights
Raw numbers and measurements Interpretation of what metrics mean
Example: "Revenue is $5M" Example: "Revenue decreased 15% due to seasonal trends"
What happened? Why it happened and what to do?

Important: Showing data without metrics can be misleading. Metrics provide context and meaning to raw numbers.

Generally, data is coming in real-time streams if you talk about OTT data or e-commerce platforms. These are 24√ó7 systems, so we are getting real-time data. There are real-time dashboards for managers, executives, and operation teams which they can use to see how visualization is changing, so they can adjust their decisions or come up with new policies.

Purpose: Monitor ongoing operations and respond to immediate issues

Key Features:

Use Case Metrics Monitored

Operational dashboards show live metrics with alerts and anomaly detection. Examples include looking at server uptime - data keeps coming in continuously. Another example is call center performance dashboards where you monitor real-time call handling metrics.

2. Analytical Dashboards

Purpose: Deep dive into data to understand patterns and root causes

Key Features:

  • Dimensional Breakdown: Analyze by category, region, product, time period

Strategic dashboards focus on high-level performance against long-term goals. The trend changes based on maybe some forecasting methods. 3.3 Design Principles for Dashboards

1. Minimalism: Less is More

Show only the required data to avoid distracting from the actual objective.

  • Remove non-essential information
  • Focus on key metrics that drive decisions
  • Avoid chart junk and decorative elements
  • Every element should serve a purpose

2. Proper Layout: Z-Pattern or F-Pattern

Human eye movement follows predictable patterns when scanning screens:

Z-Pattern (for sparse layouts):

  • Eyes move from top-left ‚Üí top-right ‚Üí diagonal ‚Üí bottom-left ‚Üí bottom-right
  • Place most important information at top-left and bottom-right
  • Good for executive dashboards with few key metrics

F-Pattern (for dense layouts):

  • Eyes move across top, then down left side, with shorter horizontal movements
  • Place critical information along left edge and top
  • Good for analytical dashboards with multiple sections
We should have proper layout - either follow Z-pattern or F-pattern for layouting these dashboards. This follows natural eye movement patterns and helps users find information quickly.

3. Strategic Use of Color

Color Guidelines:

Principle Recommendation Reason
Avoid Rainbow Colors Use limited, purposeful color palette Too many colors create visual noise and confusion
Reserve Red for Alerts Use red only for warnings, errors, critical issues Maintains urgency association; overuse desensitizes users
Cultural Considerations Consider cultural color associations Colors have different meanings in different cultures
Professional Appearance Use corporate colors or neutral palettes Maintains professional look and brand consistency
Avoid rainbow colors or maybe informal collections of colors which may not give you professional look and feel. Follow some cultural factors which we discussed in earlier lectures for designing color schemes. Use red only for alerts - this is very important.

4. Choosing Correct Charts

Chart Selection Guide:

Data Type Best Chart Why
Categorical Comparison Bar charts > Pie charts Clear height comparison vs. difficult angle estimation
Trends Over Time Line charts Shows continuous change and direction clearly
Part-to-Whole Pie charts (max 5-6 slices) Shows percentage distribution at a glance
KPIs (Trends) Sparklines Compact trend visualization within table cells
Correlations Scatter plots Shows relationship between two variables

Sparklines for KPIs:

  • Small, inline charts without axes
  • Show trend within table cells or next to numbers
  • Excellent for showing multiple KPI trends compactly
  • Example: Show revenue trend next to revenue number
If we want to compare two categories, bar charts are better compared to pie charts. Pie charts give you percentage distribution, then the rest is left on users how they perceive. But in bar charts, the clear height shows which one is low and which one is high. For trends, use line charts. For KPIs, we can use sparklines - you remember sparklines we showed in Excel? In a particular row you have some data, then in a particular cell you show the trend of that data.

5. Avoiding Clutter

  • Limit number of widgets: Don't overcrowd the canvas
  • Group related information: Use sections and containers
  • Progressive disclosure: Show summary first, details on click
  • White space: Use empty space to separate sections

6. Adding Interactivity

Essential Interactive Features:

Feature Purpose User Benefit
Filtering Select specific data subsets View only relevant information (e.g., specific time period, region)
Drill-Down Navigate from summary to details Explore root causes and detailed breakdowns
Tooltips Show additional context on hover Access detailed information without cluttering main view
Cross-Filtering Selecting in one chart filters others Understand relationships across multiple dimensions
Provide filtering widgets so that you can only select the data in which you are interested as a viewer. You can also provide drill-down widgets for more interactivity, going deeper. Provide tooltips so that you can show the required information on the visible canvas itself without cluttering it.

üí° Practice Questions

Q1: What's the difference between operational, analytical, and strategic dashboards?
Operational dashboards monitor real-time operations with live metrics and alerts. Analytical dashboards perform deep analysis to discover patterns and breakdowns by categories. Strategic dashboards focus on high-level long-term performance against organizational goals using forecasting and balanced scorecards.
Q2: Why should red color be used sparingly in dashboards?
Red should be reserved only for alerts, warnings, and critical issues to maintain its urgency association. Overusing red desensitizes users and reduces its effectiveness as an attention-grabbing signal when something truly requires immediate action.
Q3: Why are bar charts preferred over pie charts for categorical comparisons?
Bar charts have clear heights that make it immediately obvious which category is higher or lower. Pie charts require users to estimate angles and percentages, which is harder and less accurate. Bar charts provide clearer visual comparison at a glance.
Q4: What is a balanced scorecard approach in strategic dashboards?
Balanced scorecard combines forecasting with constraints and multiple perspectives - financial, customer, internal processes, and learning/growth. It provides comprehensive view of organizational performance rather than focusing only on financial metrics, enabling better strategic decision-making.
Q5: What are sparklines and when should they be used?
Sparklines are small, inline charts without axes that show trends compactly within table cells or next to KPI numbers. They're ideal for showing multiple KPI trends simultaneously in limited space, like displaying revenue trends next to revenue figures in a summary table.
