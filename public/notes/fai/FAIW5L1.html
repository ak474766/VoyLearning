1. Recap: Search in AI

The professor emphasized that in the previous module, we covered search algorithms and search problems in AI. Now we're transitioning to knowledge representation and reasoning, focusing on logical agents. The key question is: why aren't search algorithms alone sufficient to make a machine truly intelligent? Why do we need the power of reasoning and logical thinking?

Before diving into knowledge representation and reasoning, let's understand the foundational concept of how intelligent agents work. An agent always interacts with its environment through:

  • Sensors - to perceive inputs from the environment
  • Mental map - internal representation of the complex real world
  • Represented environment - simplified version of the real world for computational processing

The professor explained that as intelligent agents, we create a mental map in our heads - a representation of the real world. This complex real world is simplified into a representation that we can work with. All our calculations and decision-making happen in this represented environment (in our head), and only after we decide on our course of action do we execute it in the real world.
Yaha samjhiye ki hum sab intelligent agents hain. Hum real world ko apne dimag mein ek simple map ki tarah represent karte hain. Phir is mental map mein saare calculations karte hain, decisions lete hain, aur finally real world mein execute karte hain. Yahi process AI agents bhi follow karte hain.

Practice Questions:

  1. Q: What is the role of sensors in an intelligent agent?
    A: Sensors allow the agent to perceive inputs from the environment, similar to how humans use sensory organs to gather information from the real world.
  2. Q: Why do we need a represented environment instead of working directly with the real world?
    A: The real world is complex, so we create a simplified representation (mental map) that allows us to perform calculations and make decisions efficiently before executing actions in the real world.

Key Takeaways:

  • Intelligent agents interact with environments through sensors and actions
  • Mental maps are simplified representations of complex real-world environments
  • Decision-making occurs in the represented environment before real-world execution
  • This foundation is crucial for understanding why we need more than just search algorithms

2. Why KRR is Needed

2.1 What is Search in AI?

Search is a fundamental technique in problem-solving that involves navigating a problem space to reach a goal state.

Examples of Search Problems:

  • Solving a maze
  • Pathfinding in maps (e.g., GPS navigation)
  • Solving puzzles like 8-puzzle or Sudoku

Search works well when the following conditions are met:

  • The world is fully observable
  • Goals and actions are well-defined
  • The state space is manageable
The professor explained that in search problems, we represent the environment graphically with states and actions. We have an initial state and a goal state, and we try to find a path from the initial state to the goal state - this path is our solution. However, most real-world environments are only partially observable, not fully observable like in search problems.
Search algorithms sirf path finding ke liye acche hain - jaise maze solve karna ya GPS mein route find karna. Lekin real world mein hum sirf partially observable environment mein rehte hain, fully observable nahi. Humein sirf kuch part hi dikhta hai environment ka, poora nahi.

2.2 Limitations of Search

Search algorithms face significant limitations when dealing with complex real-world scenarios:

Knowledge That Search Cannot Handle:

  • "All humans are mortal"
  • "If fever and rash ‚Üí suspect dengue"
  • Rules and logical relationships

Key limitations of search-based approaches:

  • Path-finding focus: Not all problems are about finding paths
  • No logical reasoning: Cannot handle "what do we know?" and "what should we infer?"
  • Shallow understanding: No abstraction or deeper comprehension
  • Limited knowledge representation: Cannot encode complex relationships
The professor used a medical example: if symptoms are fever and rash, why do we suspect dengue? There must be a logical reason based on prior knowledge. We've seen many cases where fever and rash together indicate dengue. This kind of reasoning and inference cannot be handled by simple search algorithms.
Search ki limitations yeh hain ki woh sirf path finding kar sakta hai. Lekin agar humein logical reasoning karni ho - jaise fever aur rash se dengue suspect karna - toh search algorithms kaam nahi aate. Humein logic aur reasoning ki zaroorat hoti hai, jo search provide nahi kar sakta.

Practice Questions:

  1. Q: Why can't search algorithms handle statements like "All humans are mortal"?
    A: Search algorithms are designed for path-finding problems with states and actions. They cannot represent or reason about logical statements, rules, or abstract knowledge.
  2. Q: What happens when the environment is not fully observable?
    A: Search algorithms work best with fully observable environments. In partially observable environments (which is most of the real world), search algorithms struggle because they don't have complete information about all possible states and actions.

AI should not just search ‚Äî it should understand, infer, and explain. Real intelligence requires more than finding optimal paths.

Examples of Advanced AI Requirements:

Domain Required Capabilities Knowledge Needed

The professor emphasized that for medical AI, we need not just knowledge about diseases, but also about patient backgrounds, body types, geography, family medical history, and the complex relationships between symptoms and diseases. All this knowledge is essential for proper diagnosis.

Analogy: Search is like a map, but KRR is the guide who understands the terrain.

Search sirf map ki tarah hai, lekin KRR ek guide ki tarah hai jo terrain ko samajhta hai.

2.4 Why Study Knowledge Representation?

Knowledge Representation (KR) is a way to encode knowledge about the world in a form a computer can use to solve complex tasks.

Examples of KR Techniques:

  • Logic (propositional, predicate, First Order Logic)
  • Semantic networks
  • Ontologies
  • Rule-based systems

AI systems need internal representations to understand:

  • Concepts - What things are
  • Relationships - How things connect
  • Rules - How things behave
The professor mentioned that we'll be focusing on rule-based and deterministic approaches initially, not considering uncertainties in the environment. This is because we're building understanding from simple to complex tasks, similar to how a baby learns - starting with simple intelligence and gradually handling more complex scenarios.
Knowledge Representation ka matlab hai ki hum duniya ke baare mein knowledge ko aise encode karte hain ki computer use samajh sake. Logic, semantic networks, rules - yeh sab techniques hain. AI systems ko concepts, relationships, aur rules samajhne ke liye internal representation chahiye.

Key Takeaways:

  • KR encodes world knowledge for computer understanding
  • Multiple techniques exist: logic, semantic networks, ontologies
  • AI systems need to understand concepts, relationships, and rules
  • We start with deterministic approaches before handling uncertainties

2.5 Why Study Reasoning?

Reasoning is the process of drawing conclusions from knowledge. It's essential for logical decision-making.

Types of Reasoning:

Type Direction Example Application
Deductive General to specific All birds can fly
Tweety is a bird
‚à¥ Tweety can fly		 Logic systems
Inductive Specific to general Training data ‚Üí General model Machine learning
Abductive Best explanation Fever + rash ‚Üí likely dengue Diagnostics

Deductive Reasoning Example:

  • Premise 1: All birds can fly
  • Premise 2: Tweety is a bird
  • Conclusion: Therefore, Tweety can fly
The professor explained that reasoning is crucial for intelligent agents. When we speak or teach, we think logically about what our next words should be. If we were to randomly talk about unrelated topics during an AI lecture, it wouldn't follow logic. Reasoning capability is essential for any intelligent agent to make sense and follow logical patterns.
Reasoning matlab hai knowledge se conclusions nikalna. Teen types hain - deductive (general se specific), inductive (specific se general jaise machine learning mein), aur abductive (best explanation jaise medical diagnosis mein). Intelligent agent ko reasoning capability honi chahiye, warna woh pagal aadmi ki tarah behave karega.

Practice Questions:

  1. Q: Give an example of inductive reasoning in machine learning.
    A: Using specific training data examples to create a general model that can make predictions on new, unseen data.
  2. Q: Why is abductive reasoning important in medical diagnosis?
    A: It helps find the best explanation for a set of symptoms, allowing doctors to identify the most likely disease causing those symptoms.

2.6 Comparison: Search vs KRR

Aspect Search-Based AI KRR-Based AI
Focus Finding paths/solutions Understanding and reasoning
Strengths Optimal path finding, planning Handling rules, logic, structured knowledge
Weaknesses Shallow understanding, no abstraction May be slow, needs structured knowledge
Examples A* algorithm, DFS/BFS Prolog, Expert Systems, Ontologies
The professor emphasized that search is about finding optimal paths, while KRR is about handling knowledge, rules, and logic. Search algorithms like A*, DFS, and BFS are good for pathfinding, but KRR systems like Prolog and expert systems can handle complex reasoning tasks.
Search aur KRR mein main difference yeh hai ki search sirf path finding karta hai optimally, lekin KRR understanding aur reasoning karta hai. Search shallow hai, KRR deep hai. Search fast hai lekin limited, KRR slow ho sakta hai but powerful hai complex problems ke liye.

2.8 Example: Knowledge in Action

Robot Task: Make Tea

Needed Knowledge:
  • What is a kettle?
  • How to boil water?
  • What is the sequence of actions?
Needed Reasoning:
  • If water is not in kettle ‚áí fetch water
  • If kettle is not plugged in ‚áí plug it in
  • If water is not boiling ‚áí wait or increase heat

This example demonstrates how declarative knowledge (what things are) combines with procedural knowledge (how to do things) and conditional reasoning (if-then logic).

Chai banane ke liye robot ko sirf path finding nahi chahiye, knowledge chahiye ki kettle kya hai, paani kaise boil karna hai. Phir reasoning chahiye ki agar kettle mein paani nahi hai toh paani lana hai, agar plug nahi kiya toh plug karna hai. Yeh sab KRR ki madad se possible hai.

Key Takeaways:

  • Real-world tasks require both knowledge and reasoning
  • Declarative knowledge defines what things are
  • Procedural knowledge defines how to do things
  • Conditional reasoning enables dynamic decision-making

2.9 KRR + Search = Smart AI

The most powerful AI systems combine both approaches:

  • Search handles the "how" (finding optimal paths)
  • KRR provides the "what" and "why" (understanding and reasoning)

Hybrid AI Approaches:

Combining neural (sub-symbolic) and symbolic approaches:

Component Function Example
Neural Networks Pattern recognition Image recognition, speech processing
Symbolic KRR Logic, reasoning Rule-based decisions, planning
Search Algorithms Optimization, pathfinding Route planning, resource allocation

Real-World Examples:

  • IBM Watson: Combines natural language processing with knowledge reasoning
  • AlphaGo: Uses pattern recognition + strategic planning
  • Autonomous vehicles: Computer vision + route planning + rule-based safety
The professor mentioned that the next lecture will focus on symbolic representation - how to actually represent knowledge that we've been discussing. We'll explore the symbolic aspects of knowledge representation in detail.
Sabse powerful AI woh hota hai jo search aur KRR dono use karta hai. Neural networks pattern recognition karte hain, symbolic KRR logic aur reasoning karta hai, search algorithms optimization karte hain. IBM Watson, AlphaGo jaise examples mein yeh combination dekh sakte hain.

Practice Questions:

  1. Q: How does AlphaGo combine neural networks and symbolic reasoning?
    A: AlphaGo uses neural networks for pattern recognition (evaluating board positions) and combines it with strategic planning and rule-based game logic for optimal move selection.
  2. Q: Why is hybrid AI more powerful than using just search or just KRR?
    A: Hybrid AI leverages the strengths of both approaches - search provides optimal pathfinding and planning, while KRR provides understanding, reasoning, and knowledge-based decision making.
