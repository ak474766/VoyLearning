2. Prisoner's Dilemma - The Classic Example

2.1 The Scenario

The Prisoner's Dilemma is one of the most famous examples in game theory that perfectly illustrates Nash equilibrium and strategic decision-making under uncertainty. Let's understand this scenario step by step.

Setup:

  • Two prisoners: Prisoner 1 (P1) and Prisoner 2 (P2)
  • Both are held in separate cells and cannot communicate
  • Each has two possible actions/strategies:
    • Q (Quiet): Remain silent about their crimes
    • C (Confess): Confess to their crimes
  • The judge will determine jail time (payoffs) based on their choices

2.2 The Payoff Matrix

The outcomes are represented in a payoff matrix, where each cell contains an ordered pair (P1's payoff, P2's payoff). The payoffs represent years of jail time (negative values indicate time in jail):

Prisoner 1 \ Prisoner 2 Quiet (Q) Confess (C)
Quiet (Q) (-1, -1) (-12, 0)
Confess (C) (0, -12) (-8, -8)

Understanding the Payoffs:

Scenario P1's Action P2's Action Outcome
Both Quiet Q Q Both get 1 year jail time (-1, -1)
P1 Quiet, P2 Confesses Q C P1 gets 12 years, P2 goes free (-12, 0)
P1 Confesses, P2 Quiet C Q P1 goes free, P2 gets 12 years (0, -12)
Both Confess C C Both get 8 years jail time (-8, -8)

2.3 Strategic Analysis - Finding the Best Strategy

The critical question is: What should each prisoner choose? The challenge lies in the uncertainty - Prisoner 1 doesn't know what Prisoner 2 will do, and vice versa. Let's analyze this systematically.

From Prisoner 1's Perspective:

Case 1: If P1 knows that P2 will remain Quiet (Q)

P1's choices when P2 chooses Q:

  • If P1 chooses Q: Gets -1 year (1 year jail)
  • If P1 chooses C: Gets 0 years (goes free) ‚úì Better!

Result: P1 will choose C (Confess)

Case 2: If P1 knows that P2 will Confess (C)

P1's choices when P2 chooses C:

  • If P1 chooses Q: Gets -12 years (12 years jail)
  • If P1 chooses C: Gets -8 years (8 years jail) ‚úì Better!

Result: P1 will choose C (Confess)

Notice that irrespective of what P2 chooses (whether Quiet or Confess), P1's best option is always to Confess. This means Confess (C) dominates over Quiet (Q) for Prisoner 1.

From Prisoner 2's Perspective:

Case 1: If P2 knows that P1 will remain Quiet (Q)

P2's choices when P1 chooses Q:

  • If P2 chooses Q: Gets -1 year (1 year jail)
  • If P2 chooses C: Gets 0 years (goes free) ‚úì Better!

Result: P2 will choose C (Confess)

Case 2: If P2 knows that P1 will Confess (C)

P2's choices when P1 chooses C:

  • If P2 chooses Q: Gets -12 years (12 years jail)
  • If P2 chooses C: Gets -8 years (8 years jail) ‚úì Better!

Result: P2 will choose C (Confess)

2.4 The Nash Equilibrium Solution

From the above analysis, we can conclude:

  • P1's best strategy is to Confess (C) regardless of P2's choice
  • P2's best strategy is to Confess (C) regardless of P1's choice
  • Therefore, the Nash Equilibrium is (C, C) - both prisoners confess
  • At this equilibrium, both prisoners get 8 years of jail time each

‚ö†Ô∏è The Paradox

Notice something interesting: If both prisoners had chosen (Q, Q) - both remaining quiet - they would each get only 1 year of jail time, which is much better than 8 years!

However, (Q, Q) is NOT a Nash equilibrium because each prisoner has an incentive to deviate and confess to get an even better outcome (0 years).

2.5 Why Don't They Choose (Q, Q)?

The reason both prisoners end up with the suboptimal outcome (C, C) instead of the better (Q, Q) is due to lack of cooperation and communication:

Reason Explanation
No Communication Prisoners are held separately and cannot coordinate their strategies
No Trust Even if they could communicate, there's no guarantee the other will keep their promise
Individual Rationality Each prisoner acts in their own self-interest based on rational analysis
Risk Aversion If one chooses Q and the other chooses C, the outcome is catastrophic (-12 years)
This is a non-cooperative game. If the prisoners could cooperate with each other and trust each other, they may have chosen (Q, Q) strategy. But because they cannot cooperate and don't know what the other will do, both settle for the Nash equilibrium of (C, C), which gives them 8 years each instead of 1 year each.

Q1: What is the main uncertainty in the Prisoner's Dilemma?
Each prisoner doesn't know what strategy the other prisoner will choose, and they cannot communicate with each other to coordinate their decisions.
Q2: Why is (C, C) the Nash equilibrium even though (Q, Q) gives better outcomes?
At (C, C), neither prisoner can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing their strategy. However, at (Q, Q), each prisoner has an incentive to deviate to C to get an even better outcome.

The prisoner who confesses (C) goes free (0 years), while the prisoner who remained quiet (Q) gets the worst outcome of 12 years jail time.

3. Nash Equilibrium - Formal Definition

3.1 What is Nash Equilibrium?

Nash Equilibrium Definition:

In a game situation, Nash equilibrium is a situation where no player could gain more by changing their own strategy, holding all other players' strategies fixed in the game.

Let's break down this definition to understand what it really means:

Component Explanation
Game Situation A scenario where multiple players make strategic decisions that affect each other's outcomes
No Player Could Gain More Each player is already making their best possible choice given the circumstances
Changing Their Own Strategy If a player unilaterally changes their decision (acts alone)
Holding Other Players' Strategies Fixed Assuming all other players continue with their current choices

3.2 Characteristics of Nash Equilibrium

1. Stability

At Nash equilibrium, the system is stable - no player has an incentive to deviate from their current strategy. If all players are at Nash equilibrium, they will continue to stay there because moving away would make them worse off.

2. Self-Enforcing

Nash equilibrium is self-enforcing - once reached, no external enforcement is needed to maintain it. Each player voluntarily chooses their equilibrium strategy because it's in their own best interest.

3. Not Necessarily Optimal

Crucially, Nash equilibrium does NOT guarantee the optimal outcome for the group. As we saw in the Prisoner's Dilemma, both players would be better off at (Q, Q), but the Nash equilibrium is (C, C).

4. Multiple Equilibria

A game can have multiple Nash equilibria, or sometimes no Nash equilibrium in pure strategies (requiring mixed strategies).

3.3 Nash Equilibrium in Non-Cooperative Games

Nash equilibrium is particularly important and applicable in non-cooperative games, where:

  • Players cannot communicate or coordinate their strategies
  • There is no binding agreement between players
  • Each player acts independently in their own self-interest
  • Players must make decisions under uncertainty about others' choices
Nash equilibrium is a concept for non-cooperative games. In the prisoner's dilemma, the prisoners are not cooperating with each other. If they were cooperating with each other, they could have achieved a better outcome (Q, Q). But because they're not cooperating, they end up at the Nash equilibrium (C, C).

3.4 Why Nash Equilibrium Matters

Nash equilibrium is crucial in game theory and AI because it:

Importance Application
Predicts Behavior Helps predict how rational agents will behave in strategic situations
Designs Systems Used to design multi-agent AI systems and autonomous agents
Analyzes Markets Explains competitive behavior in economics and business
Resolves Conflicts Provides insight into conflict resolution and negotiation
Strategic Planning Informs strategic decision-making in competitive environments

3.5 Mathematical Representation

Formal Definition:

A strategy profile \( (s_1^*, s_2^*, \ldots, s_n^*) \) is a Nash equilibrium if for each player \( i \):

\[ u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*) \]

for all possible strategies \( s_i \) of player \( i \), where:

  • \( u_i \) = utility/payoff function for player \( i \)
  • \( s_i^* \) = equilibrium strategy of player \( i \)
  • \( s_{-i}^* \) = equilibrium strategies of all other players

üìù Hinglish Summary

Nash equilibrium ek stable situation hai jisme koi bhi player apni strategy change karke zyada benefit nahi le sakta, jab tak doosre players apni strategy same rakhte hain. Ye non-cooperative games ke liye bahut important concept hai jahan players communicate nahi kar sakte. Important baat ye hai ki Nash equilibrium hamesha optimal solution nahi deta - matlab sabke liye best outcome nahi hota. Ye concept AI systems, economics, business strategy aur conflict resolution mein bahut use hota hai.

  • It is self-enforcing and doesn't require external enforcement
  • Nash equilibrium is most relevant for non-cooperative games without communication
  • It does NOT always produce the optimal outcome for all players collectively
  • A game can have multiple Nash equilibria or none in pure strategies
  • Critical for predicting behavior in AI multi-agent systems and strategic planning

üìö Practice Questions

Q1: What makes a Nash equilibrium "stable"?
A Nash equilibrium is stable because no player has an incentive to deviate from their current strategy, assuming other players keep their strategies unchanged.
Q2: Why is Nash equilibrium important for non-cooperative games?
In non-cooperative games, players cannot communicate or coordinate, so Nash equilibrium helps predict stable outcomes where each player independently makes their best decision.
Q3: Can a Nash equilibrium be suboptimal for all players?

4. Strict Dominance Strategy

4.1 Definition of Strict Dominance

Strict Dominance Definition:

A strategy X strictly dominates strategy Y if strategy X provides a greater payoff for that player than strategy Y, regardless of what the other players do.

In simpler terms, if choosing strategy X always gives you a better outcome than choosing strategy Y, no matter what your opponents choose, then X strictly dominates Y.

4.2 Understanding Dominance

Concept Meaning Example from Prisoner's Dilemma
Dominant Strategy A strategy that is best regardless of others' choices Confess (C) is the dominant strategy
Dominated Strategy A strategy that is always worse than another Quiet (Q) is dominated by Confess (C)
Strict Dominance Dominant strategy gives strictly better payoffs (not just equal) C gives strictly better payoffs than Q in all cases
Weak Dominance Dominant strategy gives better or equal payoffs Not present in our example

4.3 How to Identify Strict Dominance

To determine if strategy X strictly dominates strategy Y for a player:

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. List all possible strategies of the other players
  2. For each opponent strategy, compare the payoffs of X and Y
  3. Check if X gives a higher payoff than Y in every single case
  4. If yes for all cases, then X strictly dominates Y
  5. The dominated strategy (Y) should never be played by a rational player

4.4 Example: Analyzing the Original Prisoner's Dilemma

Let's formally prove that Confess (C) strictly dominates Quiet (Q) in the prisoner's dilemma:

P1 \ P2 Quiet (Q) Confess (C)
Quiet (Q) (-1, -1) (-12, 0)
Confess (C) (0, -12) (-8, -8)

For Prisoner 1 (P1):

P2's Strategy P1 chooses Q P1 chooses C Better Choice
P2 chooses Q -1 year 0 years ‚úì C is better
P2 chooses C -12 years -8 years ‚úì C is better

Conclusion: Since C gives P1 a better payoff than Q in both possible scenarios (whether P2 chooses Q or C), we can conclude that C strictly dominates Q for P1.

For Prisoner 2 (P2):

By symmetry, the same analysis applies to P2. Confess strictly dominates Quiet for P2 as well.

In the prisoner's dilemma, Confess strictly dominates Quiet. That means the action C or strategy C dominates over or strictly dominates over action Q or strategy Q. This is why both prisoners will choose to confess - it's their strictly dominant strategy.

4.5 Properties of Strict Dominance

  • Rationality Assumption: A rational player will never choose a strictly dominated strategy
  • Elimination: Strictly dominated strategies can be eliminated from consideration in game analysis
  • Uniqueness: If all players have a strictly dominant strategy, the Nash equilibrium is unique
  • Simplification: Identifying dominance simplifies game analysis by reducing the strategy space

4.6 Important Note: Not All Games Have Strict Dominance

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Observation

It is not always true that every game will have strict dominance. There can be situations where:

  • No dominance exists: No strategy consistently beats another
  • Mixed dominance: For Player 1, strategy A might dominate, while for Player 2, strategy B dominates
  • Conditional dominance: One strategy is better in some scenarios but not others
  • Multiple equilibria: Games can have multiple Nash equilibria without strict dominance
In the context of this lecture, we are only talking about strict dominance. But remember, there can be scenarios where you have mixed dominance - sometimes for player 1 maybe confess is the dominant factor, whereas for player 2 quiet can be the dominant factor. Those kinds of scenarios can happen.

4.7 Strict Dominance vs. Nash Equilibrium

Aspect Strict Dominance Nash Equilibrium
Definition One strategy always beats another No player benefits from unilateral deviation
Perspective Compares strategies for a single player Considers all players' strategies together
Existence May or may not exist in a game Every finite game has at least one (in mixed strategies)
Relationship If all players have strict dominant strategies, their combination forms Nash equilibrium Nash equilibrium may exist even without strict dominance
Rationality Playing strictly dominated strategy is irrational All strategies at Nash equilibrium are rational choices

üìù Hinglish Summary

Strict dominance ka matlab hai ki ek strategy doosri strategy se hamesha better hai, chahe opponent kuch bhi kare. Jaise prisoner's dilemma mein Confess (C) strategy hamesha Quiet (Q) se better hai, isliye C strictly dominates Q. Agar kisi player ke paas strictly dominant strategy hai, to wo rational player us strategy ko definitely choose karega. Lekin dhyan rahe - har game mein strict dominance nahi hoti. Kuch games mein mixed dominance ho sakta hai ya bilkul dominance na ho. Jab sabhi players ke paas strictly dominant strategy ho, to unka combination hi Nash equilibrium banta hai.

üéØ Key Takeaways - Strict Dominance

  • Strategy X strictly dominates Y if X always gives better payoffs than Y, regardless of opponents' choices
  • Rational players never choose strictly dominated strategies
  • In the Prisoner's Dilemma, Confess strictly dominates Quiet for both players
  • Not all games have strict dominance - some have mixed or no dominance
  • When all players have strictly dominant strategies, their combination is a unique Nash equilibrium
  • Playing a strictly dominated strategy is considered irrational in game theory

üìö Practice Questions

Q1: What is the difference between strict and weak dominance?
Strict dominance means one strategy always gives strictly better payoffs. Weak dominance means one strategy gives better or equal payoffs, with at least one case being strictly better.
Q2: Why would a rational player never choose a strictly dominated strategy?
Because by definition, there exists another strategy that always gives better outcomes, so choosing the dominated strategy would be irrational and suboptimal.
Q3: If Player 1 has a strictly dominant strategy, does this guarantee Player 2 also has one?
No, each player's dominance is independent. Player 1 might have strict dominance while Player 2 might not, or they might have different types of dominance.
Q4: In the Prisoner's Dilemma, why does Confess strictly dominate Quiet?
Because choosing Confess gives a better payoff than Quiet in both possible scenarios: when the other player is Quiet (0 vs -1) and when they Confess (-8 vs -12).

5. Real-World Applications of Nash Equilibrium

Nash equilibrium and strict dominance are not just theoretical concepts - they have numerous real-world applications in military strategy, international trade, business competition, and more. Let's explore these applications in detail.

5.1 Military Conflict: Attack vs. Defend

The Scenario

Consider two countries in a potential conflict situation. Each country must decide whether to attack first or adopt a defensive posture. This represents a strategic dilemma similar to the prisoner's dilemma.

There are a lot of times we have seen that two countries are considering whether to go to war. This is also a kind of game they have to play - a mind game. They need to look at their strength and decide whether they should attack or defend.

Game Setup

Element Description
Players Country 1 and Country 2
Strategies Defend (D) or Attack (A)
Payoffs Measured in terms of losses (lives lost, resources depleted)
Key Factor Military technology gives advantage to the side that strikes first

Payoff Matrix

Country 1 \ Country 2 Defend (D) Attack (A)
Defend (D) (-3, -3) (-4, -1)
Attack (A) (-1, -4) (-2, -2)

Understanding the Payoffs

  • Both Defend (D, D): Both remain peaceful, minimal losses (-3, -3) - best collective outcome
  • Country 1 Defends, Country 2 Attacks (D, A): Country 2 has advantage of first strike, Country 1 suffers heavy losses (-4, -1)
  • Country 1 Attacks, Country 2 Defends (A, D): Country 1 has first-strike advantage, Country 2 suffers (-1, -4)
  • Both Attack (A, A): Full-scale war with mutual destruction (-2, -2)
When any country is going for war, irrespective of who is starting it first, there will be loss of lives. Either they can remain peaceful (defend), in which case nobody will lose any life, or they have to attack. Whoever is attacking first has the advantage because modern military technology gives advantage to the attacker.

Strategic Analysis

Let's determine the dominant strategy for each country:

Opponent's Strategy Country 1: Defend Country 1: Attack Better Choice
Country 2 Defends -3 -1 ‚úì Attack
Country 2 Attacks -4 -2 ‚úì Attack

Conclusion:

Attack strictly dominates Defend for both countries.

Nash Equilibrium: (Attack, Attack) with payoffs (-2, -2)

‚ö†Ô∏è Paradox: Both countries would be better off if they both chose Defend (-3, -3 is better than -2, -2), but the Nash equilibrium drives them to mutual attack!

Real-World Implications

  • Arms Races: Explains why countries engage in expensive military buildups even when peace would be better for all
  • Preemptive Strikes: Game theory shows why first-strike advantage can lead to instability
  • Deterrence: Understanding these dynamics helps design better deterrence strategies
  • International Treaties: Peace treaties can help achieve the cooperative (D, D) outcome by providing enforcement mechanisms

5.2 International Trade Wars: Tariff vs. Free Trade

The Scenario

In international trade, countries face a decision about whether to impose tariffs (taxes on imported goods) or practice free trade (no taxes). This creates a strategic situation similar to the prisoner's dilemma.

Right now there are a lot of wars - military wars. Let's talk about trade wars. Right now we can see that America is doing this trade war with all other countries. It imposes a lot of taxes on India and other nations.

Why Countries Impose Tariffs

Reason Explanation
Protect Domestic Industries Makes imported goods more expensive, giving domestic producers an advantage
Generate Revenue Tariffs bring tax revenue to the government
Negotiate Leverage Can be used as negotiating tools in trade agreements
Political Pressure Domestic industries lobby for protection from foreign competition

Trade-offs

  • Best Individual Outcome: Tax imports while other countries don't tax your exports - domestic industry protected at home, competitive abroad, plus tax revenue
  • Worst Individual Outcome: No tax on imports while other countries tax your exports - domestic industries suffer, exports reduced
  • Best Collective Outcome: Free trade for all - maximum economic efficiency and consumer welfare
  • Nash Equilibrium: Both countries tax each other - protective but inefficient

Payoff Matrix

Country 1 \ Country 2 No Tax (NT) Tax (T)
No Tax (NT) (-3, -3) (-4, -1)
Tax (T) (-1, -4) (-2, -2)

Note: Negative values represent economic inefficiency and loss of welfare. Lower absolute values are better.

Strategic Analysis

Following the same analysis as before:

  • If Country 2 chooses No Tax, Country 1 is better off choosing Tax (-1 vs -3)
  • If Country 2 chooses Tax, Country 1 is better off choosing Tax (-2 vs -4)
  • Tax strictly dominates No Tax for both countries
  • Nash Equilibrium: (Tax, Tax) with payoffs (-2, -2)
Because of this trade-off, and because Tax dominates No Tax, that is why Tax-Tax is going to be the Nash equilibrium. Both countries will tax each other, even though both would gain more from free trade.

Real-World Examples

Trade Conflict Nash Equilibrium Outcome Impact
US-China Trade War Both impose tariffs on each other's goods Higher prices for consumers, reduced trade volume
Brexit Trade Negotiations Barriers between UK and EU Reduced efficiency compared to free trade
Agricultural Subsidies Multiple countries subsidize/protect agriculture Global market distortions

How to Escape the Dilemma

  • Trade Agreements: WTO, NAFTA, EU - provide enforcement mechanisms for cooperation
  • Repeated Interactions: Long-term relationships encourage cooperation
  • International Institutions: Provide forums for negotiation and dispute resolution
  • Reputation Effects: Countries care about their reputation as reliable trade partners

Let's say two companies are competitive companies, and they are thinking about whether to go for an advertisement of a product. One may go for the advertisement, the other one may not go for the advertisement. When you are doing advertisement, you need to spend money on that also.   • Rationality: Playing a strictly dominated strategy is irrational - another strategy always yields a better outcome
  • Nash Equilibrium: The outcome where no player can improve by unilaterally changing their strategy
  • Non-optimality: Nash equilibrium does not always produce the optimal outcome for all players

6.3 Important Observations

1. The Paradox of Rationality

In all examples, individual rationality leads to collective irrationality. Each player makes the best choice for themselves, but together they achieve a worse outcome than if they had cooperated.

2. The Role of Communication and Trust

Lack of communication and trust is the root cause of suboptimal outcomes. If players could credibly commit to cooperation, they would achieve better results.

3. The Importance of Repeated Interactions

In repeated games, players can develop cooperation over time through reputation effects and the shadow of the future.

4. Institutional Solutions

Institutions, agreements, and enforcement mechanisms can help players escape prisoner's dilemmas by making cooperation enforceable and defection costly.

6.4 When Nash Equilibrium Fails to Predict

Limitation Explanation
Irrational Players Nash equilibrium assumes rational players; real humans may be emotional or bounded
Multiple Equilibria Some games have multiple Nash equilibria; theory doesn't predict which will occur
Mixed Strategies Pure strategy equilibria may not exist; players may need to randomize
Incomplete Information Players may not know payoffs or strategies available to others
Learning and Adaptation Players may learn and change strategies over time, moving toward equilibrium gradually
