2. Propositional Logic Review

2.1 Atomic and Complex Sentences

In propositional logic, we work with two fundamental types of sentences:

Atomic Sentences (Simple Sentences)

Atomic sentences are the basic building blocks of propositional logic. They are indivisible statements that can be either true or false, but not both. These are also called simple sentences or propositions.

Examples of Atomic Sentences:
  • $P$: "I am eating rice"
  • $Q$: "It is raining"
  • $R$: "The football ground is wet"

Complex Sentences (Compound Sentences)

Complex sentences are formed by connecting multiple atomic sentences using logical connectives. The structure is:

Simple Sentence + Connective + Simple Sentence = Complex Sentence

$P \land Q$ (where $\land$ is the connective)

2.2 Logical Connectives

Logical connectives are operators that combine atomic sentences to form complex sentences. Here are the primary connectives used in propositional logic:

Connective Name Symbol Read As Example
Negation (NOT) $\neg$ or $\sim$ "not" $\neg P$ (not P)
Conjunction (AND) $\land$ or $\wedge$ "and" $P \land Q$ (P and Q)
Disjunction (OR) $\lor$ or $\vee$ "or" $P \lor Q$ (P or Q)
Implication $\rightarrow$ or $\Rightarrow$ "implies" or "if...then" $P \rightarrow Q$ (if P then Q)
Biconditional $\leftrightarrow$ or $\Leftrightarrow$ "if and only if" $P \leftrightarrow Q$ (P if and only if Q)
See if we are coming to our natural language, so we can say something like "I am eating rice AND I am eating curry." Here "I am eating rice" is one atomic sentence, "I am eating curry" is another atomic sentence, and "AND" is the connective connecting them. Similarly, you could replace AND with OR, or use IF-THEN for implications.

Natural Language Examples

AND Example: "I am eating rice and I am eating curry"

‚Üí $P \land Q$ where $P$ = "I am eating rice", $Q$ = "I am eating curry"

OR Example: "I am eating rice or I am eating roti"

‚Üí $P \lor Q$ where $P$ = "I am eating rice", $Q$ = "I am eating roti"

IMPLICATION Example: "If it is raining, then I will take an umbrella"

‚Üí $P \rightarrow Q$ where $P$ = "It is raining", $Q$ = "I will take umbrella"

3. Truth Tables for Complex Sentences

A truth table is a mathematical table that shows all possible truth values for a logical expression. For complex sentences, we evaluate the truth value of the entire expression based on the truth values of its atomic components.

Fundamental Principle: Every proposition can take one of two values:
  • True (T) - symbolically denoted as 1
  • False (F) - symbolically denoted as 0

Important: If you have $n$ propositions, there will be $2^n$ possible combinations of truth values.

We said that every proposition can take two values, one is true and one is false. True itself is also a proposition which is always true. These are called tautologies, which we will talk about later.

3.1 NOT Operation (Negation)

The NOT operation (denoted by $\neg$) simply inverts the truth value of a proposition. If $P$ is true, then $\neg P$ (not P) is false, and vice versa.

$P$ $\neg P$
T F
F T
Example:
  • $P$: "I am eating rice" ‚Üí TRUE
  • $\neg P$: "I am NOT eating rice" ‚Üí FALSE

3.2 AND Operation (Conjunction)

The AND operation (denoted by $\land$) produces TRUE only when BOTH operands are true. If either operand is false, the entire expression is false.

Rule for AND: $P \land Q$ is TRUE if and only if both $P$ is TRUE AND $Q$ is TRUE. In all other cases, it is FALSE.
$P$ $Q$ $P \land Q$
T T T
T F F
F T F
F F F
Let's take an example: "I am eating rice AND I am eating curry." This sentence will be TRUE only if I am actually eating rice AND also eating curry at the same time. If I am eating rice and chapati instead, then this sentence becomes FALSE because the second part (eating curry) is false.

Real-World Understanding

Think of AND as requiring all conditions to be met. Just like to pass an exam, you might need to score 40% or more AND submit all assignments. If either condition fails, you don't pass.

3.3 OR Operation (Disjunction)

The OR operation (denoted by $\lor$) produces TRUE when AT LEAST ONE of the operands is true. It is FALSE only when both operands are false.

Rule for OR: $P \lor Q$ is TRUE if either $P$ is TRUE OR $Q$ is TRUE OR both are TRUE. It is FALSE only when both are FALSE.
$P$ $Q$ $P \lor Q$
T T T
T F T
F T T
F F F
Consider the sentence: "I will eat rice OR I will eat roti." This gives me two options. The sentence is TRUE if I eat rice, OR if I eat roti, OR even if I eat both! The sentence will be FALSE only when I am eating neither rice nor roti - when both options are not happening.

Real-World Understanding

Think of OR as requiring at least one condition to be met. Like when choosing a dessert: "I'll have ice cream OR cake" - you're happy if you get either one (or even both!).

3.4 Implication (Conditional)

The implication operation (denoted by $\rightarrow$) represents "if...then" relationships. $P \rightarrow Q$ reads as "if P then Q" or "P implies Q".

‚ö†Ô∏è Important - This is Counter-Intuitive!
$P \rightarrow Q$ is FALSE only when $P$ is TRUE and $Q$ is FALSE. In all other cases, it is TRUE!
$P$ $Q$ $P \rightarrow Q$ Explanation
T T T Promise kept: P happened and Q happened
T F F Promise broken: P happened but Q didn't
F T T No promise: P didn't happen, but Q still occurred
F F T No promise: P didn't happen, Q didn't happen
This is most unintuitive for me! Let me explain with an example: "If it rains, then the football ground will be wet." Now, this statement tells you that raining will make the ground wet, but it doesn't say that rain is the ONLY way the ground can become wet! Someone could sprinkle water on the ground too. So if it's NOT raining (P is false) but the ground is still wet (Q is true), the original statement isn't violated - it's still TRUE because there could be other causes. The statement is FALSE only when it IS raining (P is true) but somehow the ground is NOT wet (Q is false) - that would break the implication.

Why is Implication Counter-Intuitive?

Key Insight: An implication $P \rightarrow Q$ states that:
  • "Whenever P is true, Q must be true"
  • It does NOT say "P is the only way for Q to be true"
  • It does NOT say "If P is false, then Q must be false"

Example Analysis: "If it rains ($P$), then the football ground will be wet ($Q$)"

  • Case 1: It rains (T) and ground is wet (T) ‚Üí TRUE ‚úì (implication holds)
  • Case 2: It rains (T) but ground is NOT wet (F) ‚Üí FALSE ‚úó (implication violated!)
  • Case 3: It doesn't rain (F) but ground is wet (T) ‚Üí TRUE ‚úì (water sprinkler used, implication not violated)
  • Case 4: It doesn't rain (F) and ground is NOT wet (F) ‚Üí TRUE ‚úì (no claim was made about this case)

3.5 Biconditional (If and Only If)

The biconditional operation (denoted by $\leftrightarrow$) represents "if and only if" (abbreviated as "iff"). $P \leftrightarrow Q$ means $P \rightarrow Q$ AND $Q \rightarrow P$.

Rule for Biconditional: $P \leftrightarrow Q$ is TRUE if and only if both $P$ and $Q$ have the SAME truth value (both true or both false).
$P$ $Q$ $P \leftrightarrow Q$ Explanation
T T T Both true - they agree
T F F Different values - they disagree
F T F Different values - they disagree
F F T Both false - they agree
$P \leftrightarrow Q$ means both $P \rightarrow Q$ AND $Q \rightarrow P$. So biconditional is TRUE if and only if P and Q both are true or both are false. Think of it as a two-way street: P implies Q AND Q implies P. If P is true but Q is false, then one direction is violated, making the whole statement false.

Real-World Example

Statement: "You will pass the exam if and only if you score 40% or more"

  • If you score ‚â•40% ‚Üí You pass ‚úì
  • If you pass ‚Üí You must have scored ‚â•40% ‚úì
  • This is a biconditional relationship: passing and scoring ‚â•40% always go together

üåü Hinglish Summary

Truth tables complex sentences ke liye sari possible combinations dikhate hain. NOT operation values ko ulta kar deta hai. AND tab hi true hota hai jab dono conditions true hon. OR tab true hai jab kam se kam ek condition true ho. Implication ($\rightarrow$) sabse tricky hai - yeh sirf tab false hota hai jab pehla part true ho aur doosra false (promise break ho jaye). Biconditional ($\leftrightarrow$) tab true hai jab dono ki values same hon (dono true ya dono false). Yeh sab samajhna AI reasoning ke liye bahut zaroori hai!

  • With $n$ propositions, there are $2^n$ rows in the truth table

4. Inference Rules

Inference is the process of deriving new conclusions from existing statements or premises using logical reasoning. Inference rules are the formal mechanisms that allow us to make valid arguments and draw sound conclusions.

What is Inference?
  • We have a collection of statements (premises)
  • From these premises, we want to conclude something new
  • The conclusion must follow logically from the premises
  • This process creates a valid argument
Inference is about what we are inferring or concluding from given statements. We have to make valid arguments - meaning the conclusion must logically follow from the premises using proper inference rules. It's like having ingredients (premises) and following a recipe (inference rules) to cook a dish (conclusion).

4.1 Valid Arguments and Premises

An argument consists of:

  • Premises: A sequence of propositions $P_1, P_2, P_3, ..., P_n$ (given statements)
  • Conclusion: A proposition $Q$ that we want to prove

A valid argument means: If all premises are true, then the conclusion must also be true.

Valid Argument Structure:
$P_1, P_2, P_3, ..., P_n \vdash Q$

(The premises imply the conclusion)

Simple Example

Premises:
  1. "If it is raining, I will take an umbrella" ‚Üí $P \rightarrow Q$
  2. "It is raining" ‚Üí $P$
Conclusion: "I will take an umbrella" ‚Üí $Q$

This is a valid argument! We'll learn this is called Modus Ponens.

4.2 Modus Ponens

Modus Ponens (Latin for "method of affirming") is one of the most fundamental inference rules.

Modus Ponens Rule:

$$\frac{P \rightarrow Q, \quad P}{\therefore Q}$$

If we know "$P$ implies $Q$" is true, and we know "$P$" is true, then we can conclude "$Q$" is true.

Alternative notation: $(P \rightarrow Q) \land P \Rightarrow Q$

Examples

Example 1:
  • Premise 1: If it is raining, then I will take an umbrella ($P \rightarrow Q$)
  • Premise 2: It is raining ($P$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, I will take an umbrella ($Q$) ‚úì
Example 2:
  • Premise 1: If you study hard, you will pass the exam ($P \rightarrow Q$)
  • Premise 2: You studied hard ($P$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, you will pass the exam ($Q$) ‚úì

4.3 Modus Tollens

Modus Tollens (Latin for "method of denying") allows us to reason backwards from a false conclusion.

Modus Tollens Rule:

$$\frac{P \rightarrow Q, \quad \neg Q}{\therefore \neg P}$$

If we know "$P$ implies $Q$" is true, and we know "$Q$" is false, then we can conclude "$P$" must be false.

Alternative notation: $(P \rightarrow Q) \land \neg Q \Rightarrow \neg P$

Examples

Example 1:
  • Premise 1: If it is raining, the ground is wet ($P \rightarrow Q$)
  • Premise 2: The ground is NOT wet ($\neg Q$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, it is NOT raining ($\neg P$) ‚úì
Example 2:
  • Premise 1: If the alarm rings, I wake up ($P \rightarrow Q$)
  • Premise 2: I did NOT wake up ($\neg Q$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, the alarm did NOT ring ($\neg P$) ‚úì

4.4 Hypothetical Syllogism

Hypothetical Syllogism allows us to chain implications together.

Hypothetical Syllogism Rule:

$$\frac{P \rightarrow Q, \quad Q \rightarrow R}{\therefore P \rightarrow R}$$

If "$P$ implies $Q$" and "$Q$ implies $R$", then we can conclude "$P$ implies $R$".

Alternative notation: $(P \rightarrow Q) \land (Q \rightarrow R) \Rightarrow (P \rightarrow R)$

Example

  • Premise 1: If I study hard, I will understand the concepts ($P \rightarrow Q$)
  • Premise 2: If I understand the concepts, I will pass the exam ($Q \rightarrow R$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, if I study hard, I will pass the exam ($P \rightarrow R$) ‚úì

4.5 Disjunctive Syllogism

Disjunctive Syllogism uses the elimination of one option to confirm another.

Disjunctive Syllogism Rule:

$$\frac{P \lor Q, \quad \neg P}{\therefore Q}$$

If we know "$P$ or $Q$" is true, and we know "$P$" is false, then we can conclude "$Q$" must be true.

Alternative notation: $(P \lor Q) \land \neg P \Rightarrow Q$

Example

  • Premise 1: Either it is Monday or it is Tuesday ($P \lor Q$)
  • Premise 2: It is NOT Monday ($\neg P$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, it is Tuesday ($Q$) ‚úì

4.6 Addition

Addition allows us to add any proposition to a true statement using OR.

Addition Rule:

$$\frac{P}{\therefore P \lor Q}$$

If we know "$P$" is true, then "$P$ or $Q$" is also true (regardless of $Q$'s truth value).

Alternative notation: $P \Rightarrow (P \lor Q)$

Addition means you are adding something with OR, and it will not change the validity. If P is true, then "P or anything" will still be true because OR requires at least one operand to be true, and we already have P as true!

Example

  • Premise: I have a car ($P$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, I have a car OR I have a bicycle ($P \lor Q$) ‚úì

Note: This is always valid even if you don't have a bicycle!

4.7 Simplification

Simplification extracts individual components from a conjunction.

Simplification Rule:

$$\frac{P \land Q}{\therefore P} \quad \text{or} \quad \frac{P \land Q}{\therefore Q}$$

If we know "$P$ and $Q$" is true, then we can conclude both "$P$" is true and "$Q$" is true individually.

Alternative notation: $(P \land Q) \Rightarrow P$ and $(P \land Q) \Rightarrow Q$

Example

  • Premise: I have a car AND I have a bicycle ($P \land Q$)
  • Conclusion 1: Therefore, I have a car ($P$) ‚úì
  • Conclusion 2: Therefore, I have a bicycle ($Q$) ‚úì

4.8 Conjunction

Conjunction combines two true statements into one conjunction.

Conjunction Rule:

$$\frac{P, \quad Q}{\therefore P \land Q}$$

If we know "$P$" is true and "$Q$" is true separately, then we can conclude "$P$ and $Q$" is true.

Alternative notation: $P, Q \Rightarrow (P \land Q)$

Example

  • Premise 1: It is raining ($P$)
  • Premise 2: It is cold ($Q$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, it is raining AND it is cold ($P \land Q$) ‚úì

4.9 Resolution

Resolution is one of the most powerful and important inference rules, especially for automated theorem proving.

Resolution Rule:

$$\frac{P \lor Q, \quad \neg P \lor R}{\therefore Q \lor R}$$

If we have "$P$ or $Q$" and "$\neg P$ or $R$", we can "cancel out" $P$ and $\neg P$ to conclude "$Q$ or $R$".

Alternative notation: $(P \lor Q) \land (\neg P \lor R) \Rightarrow (Q \lor R)$

This is VERY VERY important! Resolution allows us to cancel out complementary literals (P and ¬¨P), similar to how we cancel terms in algebra. But don't think of it exactly like addition - it's a logical rule. This is extensively used in automated reasoning systems.

Example

  • Premise 1: Either I study OR I fail ($S \lor F$)
  • Premise 2: Either I don't study OR I get tired ($\neg S \lor T$)
  • Conclusion: Therefore, either I fail OR I get tired ($F \lor T$) ‚úì

The $S$ and $\neg S$ "resolve" or cancel out, leaving us with $F \lor T$

Worked Proof Example Using Multiple Rules

Problem: Prove $Q$ from premises $P \land (P \rightarrow Q)$
Step Statement Justification
1 $P \land (P \rightarrow Q)$ Given (Premise)
2 $P$ Simplification from step 1
3 $P \rightarrow Q$ Simplification from step 1
4 $Q$ Modus Ponens using steps 2 and 3

Conclusion: $Q$ is proved! ‚úì

Let me give you another exercise to try: Prove $Q \rightarrow R$ from these premises:
  1. $P \lor Q$
  2. $P \rightarrow (Q \rightarrow R)$
  3. $\neg P \lor (Q \rightarrow R)$
  4. $Q$
Try to work through this step-by-step using the inference rules we've learned! Each step must be justified by a valid inference rule.

5. Propositional Theorem Proving

Theorem proving is the process of establishing the truth of a statement (theorem) from given premises using logical rules. It's like cooking a dish - you're given ingredients (premises) and recipes (logical rules), and you need to produce the desired dish (conclusion).

Theorem proving means certain statements will be given to us, then we have to prove a certain statement. It's like cooking - all the materials (ingredients) will be given to you, and you have to cook a dish. But that process follows logic - you can't put water on hot oil, that's not a rule you follow while cooking, right? Similarly, when we're proving theorems, we need to follow logical rules step by step to reach our conclusion.

To effectively do theorem proving, we need to understand logical equivalences - these are transformations that preserve the truth value of expressions and allow us to manipulate logical statements systematically.

5.1 Logical Equivalence

Logical equivalence between two sentences means they have the same truth value in all possible scenarios.

Definition: Two sentences $P$ and $Q$ are logically equivalent (written $P \equiv Q$) if and only if:
  1. By Entailment: $P \vDash Q$ AND $Q \vDash P$ (they entail each other), OR
  2. By Truth Table: Their truth tables are identical for all possible truth value assignments

Notation: $P \equiv Q$ means "$P$ is logically equivalent to $Q$"

Why is Logical Equivalence Important?
  • Allows us to substitute one expression for another without changing meaning
  • Simplifies complex logical expressions
  • Essential for theorem proving and logical manipulation
  • Forms the basis for many inference techniques

5.2 Commutativity

Commutativity means we can interchange the order of operands without changing the result.

Commutative Laws:

$$P \land Q \equiv Q \land P$$ $$P \lor Q \equiv Q \lor P$$

Commutativity means we can interchange the sentences. You can check from the truth table also - if you write Q first and P after that, it should not change the truth values. The order doesn't matter for AND and OR operations.

Example

  • "I am eating rice AND curry" ‚â° "I am eating curry AND rice"
  • "It is Monday OR Tuesday" ‚â° "It is Tuesday OR Monday"

5.3 Associativity

Associativity means we can change the grouping (brackets) without changing the result.

Associative Laws:

$$(P \land Q) \land R \equiv P \land (Q \land R)$$ $$(P \lor Q) \lor R \equiv P \lor (Q \lor R)$$

Associativity is about interchanging the brackets. Here we are doing the operation of these two first (pointing to first bracket), then here you could have done it different way also - you could have done this operation first, then come to this one. The result remains the same.

Example

"(I study hard AND practice) AND review" ‚â° "I study hard AND (practice AND review)"

Both mean all three things must be done; the grouping doesn't matter.

5.4 Double Negation Elimination

Double negation elimination states that two negations cancel each other out.

Double Negation Law:

$$\neg(\neg P) \equiv P$$

"Not not P" is the same as "P"

Double negation elimination - the two negations cancel each other out. We will see in resolution also that negations can cancel out and elimination happens. This is very important for theorem proving.

Example

  • $\neg(\neg \text{"It is raining"}) \equiv \text{"It is raining"}$
  • "It is NOT true that it is NOT raining" = "It is raining"

5.5 Contraposition

Contraposition allows us to reverse and negate both parts of an implication.

Contraposition Law:

$$P \rightarrow Q \equiv \neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$$

"If P then Q" is equivalent to "If not Q then not P"

Contraposition is very very important! Please try to keep this relation in mind. $P \rightarrow Q$ is equivalent to $\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$. This is extremely useful in proofs.

Example

  • Original: "If it rains, the ground is wet" ($P \rightarrow Q$)
  • Contrapositive: "If the ground is NOT wet, then it is NOT raining" ($\neg Q \rightarrow \neg P$)
  • These are logically equivalent!

5.6 Implication Elimination

Implication elimination converts an implication into a disjunction (OR). This is one of the MOST important equivalences!

Implication Elimination Law:

$$P \rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q$$

"If P then Q" is equivalent to "Not P OR Q"

This is VERY VERY IMPORTANT! Please try to keep this relation in mind - you will be using it a lot when doing inference later on. Why is it called "elimination"? Because we are eliminating the implication symbol and replacing it with NOT and OR. Check the truth table yourself: truth table of $P \rightarrow Q$ and truth table of $\neg P \lor Q$ are exactly the same!

5.7 De Morgan's Laws

De Morgan's Laws describe how negation distributes over AND and OR operations.

De Morgan's Laws:

$$\neg(P \land Q) \equiv \neg P \lor \neg Q$$ $$\neg(P \lor Q) \equiv \neg P \land \neg Q$$

If you have done set theory, you know De Morgan's law - it's similar here also. When the NOT operation goes inside, the AND becomes OR, and OR becomes AND. Make sense? Please check for yourself using truth tables that these equivalences are true. This is a very very important rule!

Pattern Recognition

Key Pattern: When negation goes inside:
  • AND ($\land$) becomes OR ($\lor$)
  • OR ($\lor$) becomes AND ($\land$)
  • Each operand gets negated

Examples

Example 1: "It is NOT true that (I am happy AND healthy)"

‚â° "I am NOT happy OR I am NOT healthy"

$\neg(H \land L) \equiv \neg H \lor \neg L$

Example 2: "It is NOT true that (today is Monday OR Tuesday)"

‚â° "Today is NOT Monday AND today is NOT Tuesday"

$\neg(M \lor T) \equiv \neg M \land \neg T$

5.8 Distributivity

Distributivity describes how AND distributes over OR, and OR distributes over AND.

Distributive Laws:

$$P \land (Q \lor R) \equiv (P \land Q) \lor (P \land R)$$ $$P \lor (Q \land R) \equiv (P \lor Q) \land (P \lor R)$$

Distribution is about how AND is distributed over OR, or OR is distributed over AND. Please check for yourself that their truth tables are the same. This is very important for manipulating logical expressions.

Analogy to Arithmetic

This is similar to multiplication distributing over addition in arithmetic:

$a \times (b + c) = (a \times b) + (a \times c)$

Similarly in logic:

$P \land (Q \lor R) \equiv (P \land Q) \lor (P \land R)$

Example

"I will study hard AND (pass the exam OR learn something)"

‚â° "(I study hard AND pass the exam) OR (I study hard AND learn something)"

5.9 Validity and Tautologies

A sentence is valid if it is true in all possible models/interpretations. Valid sentences are also called tautologies.

Definition: A sentence is valid/tautology if it is necessarily true - true under all possible truth value assignments.

Classic Example

$$P \lor \neg P$$

This is ALWAYS true!

Take any weather of a day - "Either it will rain OR it will NOT rain." This is always true, right? In a day, either it will be raining or it is not raining. There's no third option! This will always be true, and these kinds of sentences are called tautologies. Valid sentences are necessarily true.
$P$ $\neg P$ $P \lor \neg P$
T F T
F T T

Always TRUE regardless of P's value! ‚úì

Connection to Entailment

Deduction Theorem: For any sentences $P$ and $Q$:

$P \vDash Q$ if and only if $(P \rightarrow Q)$ is valid

This connects entailment with validity and is crucial for theorem proving!

5.10 Satisfiability

A sentence is satisfiable if it is true in at least one model (at least one truth value assignment makes it true).

Definition: A sentence is satisfiable if there exists at least one model where it evaluates to TRUE.

Comparison: Validity vs Satisfiability

Property Valid (Tautology) Satisfiable
Requirement True in ALL models True in AT LEAST ONE model
Example $P \lor \neg P$ (always true) $P \land Q$ (true when both are true)
Opposite Contradiction (always false) Unsatisfiable (never true)
Satisfiability is a very very important concept! A sentence is satisfiable if it is true in, or satisfied by, some model. It doesn't have to be true in all models (that would be validity), just at least one model where it works out.

Examples

Satisfiable but not Valid: $P \land Q$

  • TRUE when $P=T, Q=T$ (satisfiable ‚úì)
  • FALSE in other cases (not valid ‚úó)

Valid (also satisfiable): $P \lor \neg P$

  • TRUE in all cases (valid ‚úì)
  • Therefore also satisfiable ‚úì

Unsatisfiable: $P \land \neg P$

  • FALSE in all cases (contradiction)
  • Not satisfiable ‚úó

5.11 Monotonicity

Monotonicity states that adding more knowledge to a knowledge base doesn't invalidate previous conclusions.

Monotonicity Property:

If $KB \vDash P$, then $(KB \cup \{Q\}) \vDash P$

If a knowledge base KB entails P, then adding any new sentence Q to KB will still entail P.

This is about enriching knowledge. Let's say we have a knowledge base KB, and from there we can imply P. Now if I add one more sentence Q to my knowledge base (enriching it), creating a new knowledge base, then this new knowledge base will also still imply P. When you enrich your knowledge, the implications that were already there will remain true - they don't change or disappear!

Understanding Through Example

Initial KB: "All humans are mortal" + "Socrates is human"

Conclusion: "Socrates is mortal" ‚úì

After adding new fact: "All humans are mortal" + "Socrates is human" + "Socrates is a philosopher"

Conclusion still holds: "Socrates is mortal" ‚úì

Adding the new fact doesn't invalidate the original conclusion!

üìù Practice Questions

Q1: Apply implication elimination to: $(P \land Q) \rightarrow R$
Using $P \rightarrow Q \equiv \neg P \lor Q$:
$(P \land Q) \rightarrow R \equiv \neg(P \land Q) \lor R$
Applying De Morgan's Law:
$\equiv (\neg P \lor \neg Q) \lor R$
$\equiv \neg P \lor \neg Q \lor R$
Q2: Simplify: $\neg(P \lor \neg Q)$
Using De Morgan's Law $\neg(P \lor Q) \equiv \neg P \land \neg Q$:
$\neg(P \lor \neg Q) \equiv \neg P \land \neg(\neg Q)$
Using Double Negation:
$\equiv \neg P \land Q$
Q3: Show that $(P \rightarrow Q) \land (Q \rightarrow P)$ is equivalent to $P \leftrightarrow Q$
By definition, biconditional $P \leftrightarrow Q$ means "$P$ implies $Q$ AND $Q$ implies $P$"
Therefore: $P \leftrightarrow Q \equiv (P \rightarrow Q) \land (Q \rightarrow P)$ ‚úì
Q4: Is $P \land (P \rightarrow Q) \rightarrow Q$ a tautology? Verify.
This represents the Modus Ponens rule. Let's check if it's always true:
For this to be FALSE, we need: $P \land (P \rightarrow Q)$ TRUE and $Q$ FALSE
If $P \land (P \rightarrow Q)$ is TRUE, then both $P$ and $(P \rightarrow Q)$ must be TRUE
But if $P$ is TRUE and $(P \rightarrow Q)$ is TRUE, then $Q$ MUST be TRUE (by definition of implication)
This contradicts our assumption that $Q$ is FALSE
Therefore, the expression is always TRUE - it's a TAUTOLOGY ‚úì
