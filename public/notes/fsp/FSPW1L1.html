Introduction to Statistics

Statistics is defined as the art of learning from data. It involves several key stages:

  • Collection: Gathering data through various methods
  • Description: Describing data in terms of measures
  • Analysis: Making calculations to understand relationships between metrics
  • Inferences: Drawing conclusions based on the analysis

Data Collection

There are primarily two approaches to data collection:

Already Available Data

  • Government published data (e.g., census data)
  • Public domain information
  • Pre-existing datasets

Design an Experiment

  • Create a controlled environment
  • Collect new data specifically for your research
  • Define parameters and variables

Example: Designing an Experiment

Research Question: "Which teaching style is more effective for a programming course?"

Group 1

Teaching Style 1

Students randomly assigned

Group 2

Teaching Style 2

Students randomly assigned

Methodology:

  1. Divide the class into two equal groups
  2. Apply Teaching Style 1 to Group 1
  3. Apply Teaching Style 2 to Group 2
  4. Record performance measures (e.g., test scores)
  5. Compare the results to determine effectiveness

Important Consideration: "The division of the groups should be done in such a manner that all possible choices of the members of a group are equally likely."

This prevents bias in the experiment. For example, if Group 1 consists of novice learners while Group 2 has students with prior programming experience, the results would be skewed by factors other than teaching style.

Data Description (Descriptive Statistics)

Once data is collected, it needs to be described and summarized. This is where Descriptive Statistics comes into play.

Methods of Data Description

  • Raw data: Scores of all students
  • Summary measures: Average of each group, median, mode, etc.
  • Graphical representations: Charts and graphs to visualize data
  • Dispersion measures: Range, variance, standard deviation

Inferential Statistics

After describing the data, we move to drawing inferences from it. This is the domain of Inferential Statistics.

  • Making predictions based on data
  • Testing hypotheses
  • Drawing conclusions about populations based on sample data
  • Determining statistical significance

In our teaching example, inferential statistics would help us determine if the difference in performance between the two groups is statistically significant or just due to chance.

Probability and the Role of "Chance"

Understanding probability is essential for making valid statistical inferences.

"To be able to draw logical conclusions from data, we usually make some assumptions about the chances (or probabilities) of obtaining the different data values."

Key Probability Concepts

  • Probability Models: Mathematical representations of random phenomena
  • Randomness: Understanding when events occur by chance
  • Statistical Significance: Determining if results are likely due to chance or a real effect

Important: Statistical inference requires some understanding of the theory of probability. This helps us determine whether observed differences are meaningful or just random variations.

Population and Samples

Population

Total collection of elements or individuals of interest for a study.

Example: All residents of a town

Sample

Subgroup of the population used for examining the elements.

Example: 100 randomly selected residents

Representative Samples

Example: Determining the average age of people in a town

LIBRARY

Recording ages of the first 100 people entering the library

Question: If the average age of these 100 people is 42 years, are we justified in concluding that this is approximately the average age of the entire population?

Answer: No. This sample is likely not representative because:

  • Library visitors may skew toward certain age groups
  • The selection is not random but based on library attendance
  • Various demographic groups may be under or over-represented

"In practice, a given sample generally cannot be assumed to be representative of a population unless that sample has been chosen in a random manner."

Representative Sampling Methods

Polling Example

Scenario: An election will be held next week and, by polling a sample of the voting population, we are trying to predict whether Party A or Party B will win.

Question: Which of the following methods of selection is likely to yield a representative sample?

  1. Poll all people of voting age attending a college basketball game.
  2. Poll all people of voting age leaving a fancy midtown restaurant.
  3. Obtain a copy of the voter registration list, randomly choose 100 names, and question them.
  4. Use the results of a television call-in poll, in which the station asked its listeners to call in and name their choice.
  5. Choose names from the telephone directory and call these people.

Best Answer: C - Obtaining a copy of the voter registration list and randomly choosing 100 names provides the most representative sample because:

  • It includes all registered voters (the actual population of interest)
  • The selection is random, eliminating selection bias
  • It doesn't favor any particular demographic group

Historical Case Study: The 1936 Literary Digest Poll

In the 1936 US Presidential election, Franklin Roosevelt defeated Alfred Landon by a landslide. However, the Literary Digest magazine predicted a Landon victory.

The Magazine's Method: They based their prediction on preferences of a sample chosen from lists of automobile and telephone owners.

Why the prediction failed: In 1936, during the Great Depression, telephone and automobile ownership was limited to wealthier Americans, who tended to favor the Republican candidate (Landon). This created a significant sampling bias.

Question for reflection: Has anything changed between 1936 and now that would make this approach work better today?

  • Telephone ownership is now nearly universal across economic classes
  • However, landline telephone directories are becoming obsolete as many people use only mobile phones
  • New biases may exist in modern telephone sampling (age demographics, unlisted numbers, etc.)
