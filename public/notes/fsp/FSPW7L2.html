Introduction to Conditional Probability

Definition & Motivation

Conditional probability is the probability of an event A given that another event B has already occurred. Formally, we write it as \( P(A \mid B) \).

This concept is used when the occurrence of one event affects the probability of another event. For example, if we know it is cloudy, the probability of rain increases compared to a random day.

Professor mentioned in class: P(A | B) means "Given event B happened, what is the chance event A will occur?".

Hinglish summary:

Key Takeaways:
  • Conditional probability is literally about updating our knowledge once something is known.
  • It allows us to "zoom in" on a part of the sample space.
  • Notation \( P(A|B) \) is standard throughout statistics.

  2. New info reduces possible outcomes, so probabilities shift accordingly.

Venn Diagram Approach

Visualizing Conditional Probability

Conditional probability can be represented by focusing on the portion of event B, and asking within B, what fraction also belongs to A.

[Insert diagram: Venn diagram showing A and B circles, intersection shaded]

The probability of A given B is given by:

\( P(A | B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \)   provided \( P(B) > 0 \).

This formula comes directly from the idea that we reduce our sample space to only where B occurs, then ask: what proportion of those are also in A?

Hinglish summary:
Agar B ho chuka hai, toh sample space B tak limited ho jata hai. Ab usme se A hone wali possibilities ka hisaab karte hain. Formula se direct nikal jaata hai: intersection ka area divided by B ka area.
Key Takeaways:
  • Venn diagram visually makes the formula intuitive.
  • Always check that \( P(B) > 0 \) – else division undefined hai.
Practice Questions:
  1. Explain the formula for conditional probability using a Venn diagram.
  2. Why must \( P(B) > 0 \) in the denominator?
Answers:
  1. The shaded overlap is the intersection – that’s what you want! Then divide by the whole of B.
  2. If \( P(B) = 0 \), then you can't condition on it because it never occurs.

Conditional Probability Formula

The Main Expression

The key formula behind conditional probability is:

\[ P(A | B)=\frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \text{ provided } P(B)>0 \]

Where:

  • P(A \cap B) is probability that A and B happen together.
  • P(B) is probability that B happens.

Sometimes, it helps to use the sample space reduction approach, especially in simple finite cases. Both methods give the same answer.

Hinglish summary:
Formula yaad rakhna: P(A | B)=P(A ∩ B)/P(B). Sample space kam ho jaata hai, toh calculation asaan ho jaati hai. Dono tarike same answer denge.
Key Takeaways:
  • Always start with intersection first, then divide by the given event's probability.
  • Both formula and reduced sample space approaches work.
Practice Questions:
  1. Write the main formula for conditional probability and explain each term.
  2. What is the role of intersection in this formula?
Answers:
  1. P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B)/P(B). It finds probability of both, then divides by probability B.
  2. Intersection shows both events happen together – that’s crucial for conditioning.

Everyday Examples of Conditional Probability

Worked Examples from Class & Slides

Die Rolling Example:
If you roll two dice, and observe that one lands on 3, the chance that their sum is 8 is calculated by listing all pairs with a 3, then finding how many of those sum to 8. Only (5,3) satisfies, so it's 1 out of 6, i.e. \( \frac{1}{6} \).

Bag of Balls:
A bag has 3 red, 2 blue balls. Draw one (not replaced), then another. Probability second is blue given first was red? Remove 1 red, leaving 2 blue out of 4 balls: \( \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2} \) using reduced sample space.

Deck of Cards:
Draw one card (it's a king), then draw another. Probability second is king? Only 3 kings remain out of 51 left, so \( \frac{3}{51} \).

Transistors:
From a bin of 5 defective, 10 partially defective, 25 acceptable, pick one that doesn’t immediately fail. Probability it’s acceptable? Acceptable/non-defective = \( \frac{25}{40} / \frac{35}{40} = \frac{5}{7} \).

Father-Son Dinner Problem:
Jones has two kids and is invited (means at least one son). Probability both kids are boys? Sample space shrinks to only choices with at least one boy: probability is \( \frac{1}{3} \).

Practice Questions:
  1. From a deck of 52 cards, 4 are kings. If you draw one king first, what's the chance the second card is also a king?
  2. Bag contains 3 red, 2 blue balls. Given first drawn is red, what’s the chance second is blue?
  3. Father-son dinner: If Jones is invited, what is the probability both kids are boys out of all possible combinations?
Answers:
  1. 3 remaining kings out of 51; answer is \( \frac{3}{51} \).
  2. 2 blue balls out of 4 remaining; answer is \( \frac{1}{2} \).
  3. Possible combinations are 3 out of 4, so answer is \( \frac{1}{3} \).
