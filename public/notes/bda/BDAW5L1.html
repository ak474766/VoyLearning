1. Introduction to Data Sources

In the era of the Internet revolution, data sources have become incredibly diverse and widespread. Never before in history have we had access to such a vast array of data types and collection methods. This transformation has fundamentally changed not only how we collect and store data, but what we consider to be data itself.

Traditionally, data sources were limited to numeric or quantitative data - numbers in spreadsheets and databases. However, today's data landscape is much broader. With the Internet revolution, data revolution, and artificial intelligence revolution, almost any form of information about human behaviour, human choices, and human experiences can now be considered data.

Professor mentioned in class: "This is one of those areas where you get to show some creativity in your data analytics journey. Very often, new, innovative, uncommon sources of data can give you more insight and give you an edge over other data analysts."

We now have a plethora of tools at our disposal that can be used to process various types of information to obtain valuable insights. This lecture explores where data actually comes from, examining both common sources and different types of data sources.

2. Common Sources of Data

There are numerous sources from which data can be collected in modern data analytics. While this lecture covers the most common ones, data analysts often encounter many more specialized sources throughout their careers. Let's explore each major category in detail:

2.1 Relational Databases

Relational databases are structured systems where data is organized into tables with defined relationships between them. Every individual unit in a relational database is connected to other units through common identifiers, which is what makes them "relational."

Business Applications

Most businesses maintain internal databases to organize their workflows across different departments and functions. Each business activity may have its own database:

Database Type Purpose Example Use Case
Business Activities Database Track daily operations and processes Recording production schedules, inventory movements
Customer Transactions Database Store all customer purchase records Sales projections for next quarter
Human Resource Database Maintain employee information Employee appraisals and promotion decisions

These databases can be used individually for specific analyses or collectively as interconnected systems. An overarching database often connects all departmental databases in terms of workflows, creating a comprehensive data ecosystem.

Popular Database Software

Businesses commonly use the following software systems to manage and query relational databases:

  • SQL (Structured Query Language) - The most fundamental and widely-used database query language
  • Oracle - Enterprise-level relational database management system
  • MySQL - Open-source relational database system
  • IBM DB2 - IBM's enterprise database solution

Key Characteristics

Relational databases have several defining characteristics:

  • Store data in a structured way with rigid, predefined schemas
  • All generated data is plugged into existing structures
  • Data is stored until queried for analysis
  • Can connect multiple databases for complex analysis
  • Enable sophisticated queries across related tables
Professor mentioned in class: "One of the important tasks of a data analyst is querying or gathering data. You will learn SQL and other query languages in later lectures and lab components to master this essential skill."

Real-World Examples

Here are practical examples of how businesses use relational databases:

  • Sales Forecasting: A business might connect customer transaction database with business activities database to make accurate sales projections for the next quarter
  • Performance Management: HR departments use employee data from the human resource database along with workflow tracking data to make informed decisions about employee appraisals and promotions
  • Inventory Optimization: Connecting sales data with supply chain databases to optimize stock levels

2.2 Flat Files

Flat files are standalone data files that contain information on specific topics. Unlike relational databases maintained internally by businesses, flat files are often external data sources that organizations can access and use for their analyses.

Sources of Flat Files

Flat files are commonly published by various organizations:

Source Organization Type of Data Use Cases
Governments Economic and demographic datasets Understanding market conditions, population trends
NGOs Social and environmental data Market research, social impact analysis
Commercial Data Providers Industry-specific data (weather, sales, housing) Business planning, forecasting operations

Common Types of Flat Files

Flat files are available in various formats, each serving different purposes:

  • Point of Sale (POS) Data: Information on items sold, helping businesses understand consumer purchasing patterns
  • Weather Data: Meteorological information that companies use to forecast or plan operations (e.g., agricultural businesses, event planning)
  • Housing Price Data: Real estate market information providing insights into housing market trends
  • Economic Indicators: GDP, inflation rates, employment statistics
Professor mentioned in class: "These flat files are actually often useful to other businesses or even to small businesses or content creators to understand their market, understand the projection of where the economy is going, and various other things."

File Formats

Flat files are typically shared in several standard formats (you'll learn more about these in a later lecture on data formats):

Format Description Characteristics
Spreadsheets Excel, Google Sheets files Can contain multiple sheets, each mapping to a single table
Delimited Files CSV (Comma-Separated Values), TSV (Tab-Separated Values) Plain text format, each row = one record, maps to single table
XML Files Extensible Markup Language Can support slightly more complex data structures

Data Structure in Flat Files

Flat files typically follow a structured format:

  • Store data in plain text format (though some may use additional formats)
  • Each row or line represents one record
  • In delimited files, all data maps to a single table
  • In spreadsheets, multiple sheets can exist, but relationships between sheets may or may not be defined
  • Follow a rigid structure with consistent formatting

Business Applications

Businesses use flat files for various strategic purposes:

  • Creating Business Strategies: Using external economic data to inform strategic planning
  • Predicting Demand: Analyzing historical sales data and market trends
  • Planning Supply Chains: Using weather and economic data to optimize logistics
  • Market Analysis: Understanding demographic trends for targeted marketing

Q2: List three common formats in which flat files are shared.

Answer: Three common formats are: (1) Spreadsheets (like Excel or Google Sheets), (2) Delimited files (CSV - Comma-Separated Values, TSV - Tab-Separated Values), and (3) XML files (Extensible Markup Language).

2.3 APIs and Web Services

APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) and web services are powerful, dynamic sources of data that allow multiple users and applications to interact with them to obtain information. They represent a modern, efficient way of accessing data on demand.

What Are APIs?

An API is a set of protocols and tools that allows different software applications to communicate with each other. In the context of data analytics:

  • APIs accept incoming requests (from users via web browsers or from applications via network calls)
  • They process these requests
  • They return data in your preferred format (plain text, XML, JSON, etc.)

Popular APIs for Data Analytics

API Type Examples Data Provided Common Use Cases
Social Media APIs Twitter API, Facebook API Text data from tweets, posts, comments Customer sentiment analysis, brand monitoring
Financial APIs Stock Market APIs (NYSE, NASDAQ) Real-time stock prices, trading volumes, transactions Financial analysis, algorithmic trading, economic monitoring
Data Validation APIs Email validation, address verification Data quality metrics, verification results Data cleaning, quality assurance
Lookup APIs Geocoding, company information Geographic coordinates, business details Location-based analysis, company research

Social Media APIs - Deep Dive

Social media platforms provide APIs that enable data analysts to extract valuable information:

  • Twitter/Facebook APIs: Extract text data from tweets, posts, and comments
  • Analysis Application: Use Natural Language Processing (NLP) to analyze customer sentiments
  • Business Value: Understand how customers perceive products, services, or brands

A business can apply natural language processing tools on text data retrieved through APIs to understand customer sentiments."

    • Transaction volumes
    • Stock prices

    • Trading patterns

2.4 Web Scraping

Web scraping, also known as screen scraping, web harvesting, or web data extraction, is the process of extracting relevant data directly from websites. While APIs provide structured access to data through formal requests, web scraping allows you to extract information from web pages yourself.

What Is Web Scraping?

Web scraping involves:

  • Extracting specific data based on defined parameters
  • Automatically gathering information from multiple web pages
  • Collecting data that may not be available through APIs
  • Building custom datasets from publicly available web content
Professor mentioned in class: "Most of the time, the data that we get through web scraping is unstructured. This process of web scraping is very versatile - it can extract data from texts, quantitative data, and even image data."

Types of Data Extracted Through Web Scraping

Data Type Examples Applications
Text Data Product descriptions, reviews, articles Sentiment analysis, content aggregation
Quantitative Data Prices, ratings, statistics Price comparison, market analysis
Image Data Product photos, user-generated images Visual analysis, catalog building

Popular Use Cases for Web Scraping

1. E-commerce Price Monitoring

One of the most common applications of web scraping is in the e-commerce sector:

  • Price Comparison: Track how prices for the same product vary across different platforms (Amazon, Walmart, eBay, etc.)
  • Product Details: Extract product characteristics, specifications, and features
  • Competitive Analysis: Understand how competitors are pricing their products
  • Market Research: Identify trends in product offerings and pricing strategies
Professor mentioned in class: "Obviously, if you are shopping, you go through these product details one by one. But if you're analyzing, you need to analyze large volumes of these product details data. Web scraping is a way to pull all of that data that is listed on e-commerce platforms."
2. Discussion Forums and Online Communities

Online communities are rich sources of qualitative data:

  • Question-Answer Forums: Platforms like Stack Overflow, Reddit, Quora
  • Product Review Sites: Communities where users discuss product features and experiences
  • Customer Feedback: Understanding how customers perceive products
  • Problem Identification: Discovering common issues or questions users have

This data provides valuable insights into customer opinions and can help companies improve their products and services.

3. Job Market Analysis

Job postings across various portals provide insights into the economy:

  • Employment Trends: What types of jobs are in demand
  • Skill Requirements: What skills employers are looking for
  • Salary Information: Compensation trends across industries
  • Economic Indicators: Understanding the health of the job market
  • Geographic Patterns: Where hiring is concentrated

Governments and businesses use this scraped job posting data to understand the state of the economy and labor market at any given time.

2.5 Data Streams and Feeds

Data streams and data feeds represent continuous flows of data that are constantly being generated and updated in real-time. Unlike static databases or flat files, these sources provide dynamic, time-sensitive information that changes moment by moment.

What Are Data Streams?

Data streams are continuous flows of data from sources that are constantly monitoring or recording information:

  • IoT Devices: Internet of Things devices and smart sensors
  • GPS Systems: Location tracking from cars, phones, and other mobile devices
  • Social Media: Continuously updated timelines as users post content
  • Websites and Applications: Constantly receiving and processing user interactions
  • Instruments and Monitors: Industrial sensors, medical equipment, weather stations
Professor mentioned in class: "Because this data is constantly changing and updating, data harvested through data streams and data feeds often comes with time stamps and geo tags for identification. We want to know where and when this data came to be."

Characteristics of Data Streams

  • Real-time: Data is generated and transmitted continuously
  • Time-stamped: Each data point includes when it was generated
  • Geo-tagged: Location information often included
  • High Volume: Large amounts of data generated quickly
  • Temporal Relevance: Data value may decrease over time

Popular Examples of Data Streams and Feeds

1. Stock Market Tickers

Financial markets generate continuous streams of data:

  • Track stock prices in real-time
  • Monitor trading volumes
  • Provide instant updates on market movements
  • Used by financial analysts and stock traders for decision-making
  • Essential for algorithmic trading systems
2. Retail Transaction Streams

Global retail chains generate continuous transaction data:

  • As long as stores are open, customers are making purchases
  • Real-time sales data across multiple locations
  • Applications:
    • Predicting customer demand
    • Supply chain management
    • Inventory optimization
    • Determining which products need restocking
Professor mentioned in class: "A company might be interested in harvesting this data on transactions coming in through data streams to understand the demand of customers and decide which product they need to stock up more on or which product they need to get more inventory for."
3. Surveillance and Video Feeds

Continuous video monitoring for security purposes:

  • Airports constantly monitoring for security threats
  • Real-time threat detection
  • Facial recognition systems

4. Social Media Feeds

Social platforms generate massive real-time data:

  • New posts, tweets, and reels posted every second
  • Real-time sentiment analysis
  • Trend detection (hashtags, viral content)
  • Event monitoring

That is a good example of analysis of data streams and data feeds. As many people talk about a given issue, we can quickly analyze all of that data coming in through their posts and tweets, and then it becomes a trending topic."

Modern devices have sensors constantly collecting data:

  • Machinery performance monitoring
  • Temperature and environmental sensors

  • Wearable fitness trackers
6. Web Click Streams

Websites monitor user interactions in real-time:

Travel and reservation platforms process continuous updates:

2.6 Satellite Imagery

Beyond the common sources discussed, data analysts may need to explore novel and specialized sources of data depending on their specific needs. Satellite imagery is one such powerful but underutilized source that provides unique insights impossible to obtain from ground-level data collection.

Overview of Satellite Data

Almost every country has satellites orbiting Earth, capturing images and data for various purposes. These satellites provide a bird's-eye view of the planet, enabling large-scale monitoring and analysis.

Types of Satellite Imagery

1. Daytime Imagery

Photographs of Earth taken during daylight hours have numerous applications:

Application Area Use Case Who Uses It
National Security Monitoring buildings, infrastructure, potential threats Government agencies, defense departments
Environmental Monitoring Tracking deforestation, forest health Forest agencies, environmental organizations
Weather Forecasting Cloud patterns, storm systems Meteorological departments
Agriculture Crop monitoring, land use analysis Agricultural departments, farming companies
2. Weather and Environmental Monitoring

Satellites continuously track meteorological and environmental data:

  • Temperature: Surface and atmospheric temperature measurements
  • Rainfall: Precipitation patterns and prediction
  • Pollution Levels: Air quality and pollutant tracking
  • Cloud Cover: Weather system movement
Professor mentioned in class: "The weather forecast that we get through the news on our phones every day, which is fairly accurate, is data that is coming from these satellites. This is one area that has seen an explosion of data analytics over the past few decades. Our weather forecasts have become very, very accurate over the last few years, mainly because of our ability to process large volumes of data very quickly."

The professor further explained:

"Weather patterns can change very fast. So if data processing time takes too long, by the time we process the data, the weather could have changed. Satellite data is often used to track weather patterns, and we can get very localized information on weather patterns now."
3. Nighttime Imagery

Photographs of Earth taken at night reveal fascinating economic and social patterns:

  • Population Density: Lit areas indicate human settlements and their density
  • Economic Activity: Brightness of lights correlates with economic development
  • Urban Growth: Tracking expansion of cities over time
  • Energy Consumption: Understanding power usage patterns
  • Development Indicators: Identifying areas with infrastructure vs. those without

4. Population vs Sample Data

When intentionally collecting data, one of the most critical decisions is whether to collect data from the entire population or from a representative sample. Understanding this distinction is fundamental to effective data collection and analysis.

Defining Population and Sample

Population Data

Population data refers to the complete set of data you want to study‚Äîthe entire group of interest.

  • Includes every single member of the group you're studying
  • Represents the actual, complete dataset
  • No estimation needed‚Äîyou have all the information

Example: All customers who have ever purchased a product from your company would be your target population if you want to study customer behavior.

Sample Data

Sample data is a subset of the population taken in a way that it represents the entire population.

  • Includes only some members of the population
  • Must be selected carefully to represent the whole population
  • Used to make inferences about the entire population
  • More practical and feasible than population data in most cases

Large Circle (Population - All Customers) ‚Üí Arrow pointing to ‚Üí Smaller Circle (Sample - Selected Representative Customers)

Why Use Sampling?

There are several compelling reasons why sampling is often necessary:

Reason Explanation Example
Cost Collecting data from entire population is expensive Surveying 100,000 customers vs. 1,000 customers
Time Processing all data takes too long Analyzing data from millions takes months
Impossibility Can't reach or access everyone All potential customers (including future ones)
Destruction Testing destroys the product Quality testing candies or lightbulbs

The Candy Factory Example

Professor mentioned in class: "Suppose you are working as a data analyst in a candy factory or a candy company, and you need to understand the quality of the candy that is being produced. In order to do that, you will have to collect data by taking the candy and sampling the candy. But you cannot possibly sample all of the candy that the company has produced, because in that process you will actually destroy their entire production, and not to mention the fact that you cannot possibly eat so much candy."

This example perfectly illustrates the concept of destructive testing‚Äîwhere the act of testing consumes or destroys the item being tested. Other examples include:

  • Crash testing vehicles for safety
  • Testing the lifespan of lightbulbs
  • Pharmaceutical drug testing
  • Material stress testing

Benefits of Sampling

Professor mentioned in class: "Sampling allows us to gather insights about the population while only analyzing a part of that entire data."
  • Cost-Effective: Dramatically reduces data collection and processing costs
  • Time-Efficient: Enables faster data collection and analysis
  • Practical: Makes infeasible projects feasible
  • Quality Control: Allows for more thorough analysis of smaller dataset
  • Resource Optimization: Better allocation of limited resources

Critical Considerations for Sampling

When sampling, two crucial factors must be carefully considered:

1. Sample Size

The number of individuals included in your sample is critical:

  • Too Small: May not provide enough information for reliable conclusions
    • Higher margin of error
    • Less confidence in results
    • May miss important patterns
  • Too Large: Defeats the purpose of sampling
    • Increased cost
    • More time required
    • Resource intensive
  • Just Right: Balances statistical reliability with practical constraints
    • Adequate for reliable conclusions
    • Cost-effective
    • Time-efficient
Professor mentioned in class: "More data is better, but if you try to take too much of the sample, you are running into the same problem as you would face if you were trying to work with the population. Taking a very large sample will increase the cost of collecting data and increase the time needed to process the data."

2. Representativeness

The sample must accurately reflect the characteristics of the entire population:

Professor mentioned in class: "It is important to ensure that the sample is actually representative of the population."
The Clothing Brand Example

Consider a clothing brand wanting to survey customers about product satisfaction and preferences:

  • Customer Diversity: Customers don't all have the same tastes and preferences
  • Geographic Spread: Customers may be spread across different regions with different climate needs
  • Purchase Timing: Customers buy at different times (seasonal variations)
  • Product Preferences: Different customers prefer different types of clothing

Non-Representative Sampling Would Be:

  • Surveying customers from only one geographic area
  • Collecting responses only from recent purchases
  • Including only customers who bought one type of product
  • Sampling only during one season

Representative Sampling Would Include:

  • Customers from all geographic regions where products are sold
  • Customers who purchased at different times (across seasons)
  • Customers who bought various types of clothing
  • Mix of frequent and occasional customers

Consequences of Poor Sampling

Professor mentioned in class: "Having nonrepresentative samples can lead you to make wrong conclusions about the data."

Problems that arise from poor sampling:

  • Sampling Bias: Systematic error in sample selection
  • Incorrect Conclusions: Decisions based on unrepresentative data
  • Wasted Resources: Money and time spent on flawed research
  • Business Errors: Wrong strategic decisions
  • Loss of Credibility: Stakeholders lose trust in analysis

Advanced Statistical Methods for Sampling

Professor mentioned in class: "There are many advanced statistical methods that allow you to correctly sample the data to understand what is the right sampling strategy for a given problem."

Common sampling methods include:

Sampling Method Description When to Use
Simple Random Sampling Every member has equal chance of selection Homogeneous populations
Stratified Sampling Population divided into strata, then sampled Diverse populations with distinct groups
Cluster Sampling Population divided into clusters, sample entire clusters Geographically dispersed populations
Systematic Sampling Every nth member selected When population list is available

Key Principles to Remember

  1. Clear Objectives: Know exactly what you want to learn from the data
  2. Define Population: Clearly identify the entire group you want to understand
  3. Determine Sample Size: Use statistical formulas or rules of thumb
  4. Ensure Representativeness: Sample should mirror population characteristics
  5. Avoid Bias: Use proper randomization techniques
  6. Document Method: Record how sampling was done for transparency
